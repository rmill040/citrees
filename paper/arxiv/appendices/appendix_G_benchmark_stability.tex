\section{Benchmark stability and configuration sensitivity}
\label{app:benchmark-stability}

This appendix reports two additional analyses for the real-data benchmark in
the main text. It gives the paired CIF-versus-baseline results on the
14-dataset classification benchmark and the leave-one-dataset-out
configuration check.

\subsection{14-dataset classification benchmark}

\Cref{tab:fixed-panel-cif-ci} gives the paired CIF-versus-baseline comparisons
for the three historical conditional-inference baselines on the 14-dataset
classification benchmark, using the same 14 datasets for all downstream
models and for $k = 5, 10, 25, 50$, and $100$.

  \setlength{\tabcolsep}{5pt}
  \begin{tabular}{@{}l r l c c@{}}

    \midrule
    \texttt{r\_ctree} & 0.109 & [0.068, 0.154] & 14--0--0 & $<0.001$ \\
    \texttt{r\_cforest} & 0.111 & [0.067, 0.161] & 13--1--0 & $<0.001$ \\
    \texttt{cit} & 0.041 & [0.023, 0.060] & 13--1--0 & $<0.001$ \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
  \caption{Paired CIF-minus-baseline balanced-accuracy deltas on the
  14-dataset classification benchmark. The confidence intervals are percentile
  bootstrap intervals from 20{,}000 replicates.}
  \label{tab:fixed-panel-cif-ci}

The same 14-dataset classification benchmark yields a Friedman test statistic
of
$\chi^2(14)=125.73$ with $p<0.001$ and Kendall's
$W=0.64$. For the smaller 6-dataset regression benchmark with complete
coverage, the
corresponding omnibus summary is $\chi^2(15)=29.22$, $p=0.0150$, and
Kendall's $W=0.32$, which is why the paper reports regression only as a
smaller secondary result.

\subsection{Mechanism scaling by number of features}

\Cref{fig:mechanism-dimension-curves} extends the main-text mechanism figure
from one sparse classification setting to $p = 100, 500$, and $1000$ at 1000
trees. For each method and number of features, the point marks the mean over
$n_{\mathrm{informative}} \in \{1,2,5,10\}$ and the vertical segment shows the
range over those four sparse settings.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/paper_mechanism_grid_forest_classification_dimension_curves_1000trees.png}
  \caption{Classification forest mechanism scaling by number of features at
  1000 trees. Points are means over
  $n_{\mathrm{informative}} \in \{1,2,5,10\}$ and vertical segments show the
  min--max range over those sparse designs.}
  \label{fig:mechanism-dimension-curves}
\end{figure}

The gap between CIF and \texttt{cif\_all} widens as the number of features
grows and is largest in the sparsest settings. With two informative features,
the difference in informative split share is 0.600 at $p{=}100$, 0.780 at
$p{=}500$, and 0.910 at $p{=}1000$, while the corresponding reductions in the
number of different false features used by the forest are 70, 264, and 439.
The same pattern persists when the signal is less sparse: with five
informative features, the difference in informative split share is still
0.251, 0.564, and 0.543 as $p$ increases from 100 to 500 to 1000. These
curves support the main-text conclusion that, in sparse high-$p$ forests,
subsampling often keeps informative features out of CIF's candidate set.
