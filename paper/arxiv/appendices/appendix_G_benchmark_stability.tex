\section{Benchmark stability and candidate feature sampling}
\label{app:benchmark-stability}

This appendix reports additional results that support the main text: matched
CIF comparisons on the classification benchmark with 14 datasets, the
leave-one-dataset-out (LODO) configuration check, and a candidate feature
sampling analysis by number of features.

\subsection{Classification benchmark with 14 datasets}

\Cref{tab:fixed-panel-cif-ci} gives the matched CIF comparisons
against ctree, cforest, and CIT. All comparisons use the same 14 datasets, the
same downstream models, and $k \in \{5,10,25,50,100\}$.

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \footnotesize
  \setlength{\tabcolsep}{5pt}
  \begin{tabular}{@{}l r l c c@{}}
    \toprule
    Compared method & Mean $\Delta$ & 95\% CI & W--L--T & Sign test $p$ \\
    \midrule
    ctree & 0.109 & [0.068, 0.154] & 14--0--0 & $<0.001$ \\
    cforest & 0.111 & [0.067, 0.161] & 13--1--0 & $<0.001$ \\
    CIT & 0.041 & [0.023, 0.060] & 13--1--0 & $<0.001$ \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
  \caption{Matched balanced accuracy differences between CIF and the compared
  method on the classification benchmark with 14 datasets. The confidence intervals
  are percentile bootstrap intervals from 20{,}000 replicates. Positive
  differences favor CIF.
  W--L--T gives the number of datasets where CIF wins, loses, or ties against
  the compared method.}
  \label{tab:fixed-panel-cif-ci}
\end{table}

The same classification benchmark with 14 datasets yields a Friedman test statistic
of
$\chi^2(16)=141.32$ with $p<0.001$ and Kendall's
$W=0.63$. For the smaller regression benchmark with 6 datasets and complete
coverage, the
corresponding omnibus summary is $\chi^2(17)=33.79$, $p=0.0089$, and
Kendall's $W=0.33$, which is why the paper reports regression only as a
smaller secondary result.

\subsection{Candidate feature sampling by number of features}

\Cref{fig:mechanism-dimension-curves} extends the main text candidate feature
sampling figure from one sparse classification setting to $p = 100, 500$, and
$1{,}000$ at 1,000 trees. For each method and number of features, the point
marks the mean over $n_{\mathrm{informative}} \in \{1,2,5,10\}$ and the
vertical segment shows the range over those four sparse settings.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/paper_mechanism_grid_forest_classification_dimension_curves_1000trees.png}
  \caption{Classification forest candidate feature sampling by number of
  features at 1,000 trees. Points are means over
  $n_{\mathrm{informative}} \in \{1,2,5,10\}$ and vertical segments show the
  min--max range over those sparse designs.}
  \label{fig:mechanism-dimension-curves}
\end{figure}

The gap between CIF and CIF with all features available widens as the number of
features grows and is largest in the sparsest settings. With two informative features,
the difference in informative split share is 0.600 at $p{=}100$, 0.780 at
$p{=}500$, and 0.910 at $p{=}1{,}000$, while the corresponding reductions in the
number of different uninformative features used by the forest are 70, 264,
and 439.
The same pattern persists when the signal is less sparse: with five
informative features, the difference in informative split share is still
0.251, 0.564, and 0.543 as $p$ increases from 100 to 500 to 1,000. These
curves support the main text conclusion that, in sparse high-$p$ forests,
subsampling can make informative features unavailable at too many split
decisions.
