\section{Candidate feature availability}
\label{app:candidate-availability}

This appendix gives the extended $p$-sweep behind the main-text
candidate feature sampling diagnostic, separate from the benchmark-stability
summaries in \cref{app:benchmark-stability}.

\Cref{fig:candidate-dimension-curves,fig:regression-candidate-dimension-curves}
show the candidate feature sampling diagnostic at feature dimensions
$p \in \{100,500,1{,}000\}$, using forests with 1,000 trees per fit, separately
for classification and regression. For each method and feature dimension $p$,
the point marks the mean over
$n_{\mathrm{informative}} \in \{1,2,5,10\}$, and the capped bar spans the
minimum and maximum over those four sparse settings.

In classification, CIF-all separates most clearly from default CIF in sparse
high-$p$ settings. CIF-all evaluates all nonconstant candidate features at each
split; feature muting and the other tree settings are unchanged. With two
informative features and $p{=}100$, default CIF uses informative features in
40.0\% of splits, while CIF-all does so in 100.0\%. The corresponding values
are 12.2\% versus 90.2\% at $p{=}500$ and 9.0\% versus 100.0\% at
$p{=}1{,}000$. CIF-all also uses 70, 264, and 439 fewer distinct uninformative
features than default CIF. With five informative features, the CIF-all-minus-CIF
differences are 25.1, 56.4, and 54.3 percentage points at $p{=}100$,
$p{=}500$, and $p{=}1{,}000$.

Regression shows a similar pattern in the one- and two-informative-feature
settings, but CIF-all also uses more distinct uninformative features. With two
informative features and $p{=}100$, default CIF uses informative features in
39.9\% of splits, while CIF-all does so in 58.8\%. The corresponding values are
19.7\% versus 46.7\% at $p{=}500$ and 11.6\% versus 35.9\% at
$p{=}1{,}000$. With one informative feature, the CIF-all-minus-CIF differences
are 29.8, 18.9, and 42.1 percentage points.
