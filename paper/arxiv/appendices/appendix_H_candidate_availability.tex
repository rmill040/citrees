\section{Candidate feature availability}
\label{app:candidate-availability}

This appendix reports the extended candidate feature availability analysis separately
from the benchmark-stability summaries in \cref{app:benchmark-stability}.

\Cref{fig:candidate-dimension-curves,fig:regression-candidate-dimension-curves}
extend the main text candidate feature sampling figure from one sparse setting
to $p = 100, 500$, and $1{,}000$ at 1,000 trees, separately for classification
and regression. For each method and number of features, the point marks the mean
over $n_{\mathrm{informative}} \in \{1,2,5,10\}$ and the capped bar spans the
minimum and maximum over those four sparse settings.

In classification, the gap between default CIF and CIF-all widens as the number
of features grows and is largest in the sparsest settings. CIF-all is the CIF
variant with \texttt{max\_features=None}, so every nonconstant candidate feature
can enter Stage~A at each split; feature muting and all other tree settings are
held fixed. With two informative features, the difference in informative split
share is 0.600 at $p{=}100$, 0.780 at $p{=}500$, and 0.910 at $p{=}1{,}000$,
while the corresponding reductions in distinct uninformative features used by
the forest are 70, 264, and 439. With five informative features, the split-share
differences are still 0.251, 0.564, and 0.543 as $p$ increases.

Regression shows the same candidate-availability effect for sparse signals, but
with a less one-sided tradeoff because CIF-all uses more distinct uninformative
features. With two informative features, CIF-all raises the informative split
share by 0.189, 0.269, and 0.243 as $p$ increases from 100 to 1,000; with one
informative feature, the gains are 0.298, 0.189, and 0.421.
