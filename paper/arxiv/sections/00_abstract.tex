Conditional inference trees (CIT) and conditional inference forests (CIF)
reduce split selection bias by testing candidate split features before choosing
split thresholds, but repeated permutation tests and threshold searches make
that separation computationally expensive. We study CIT and CIF as feature
selection methods and ask how selected runtime controls affect the resulting
rankings. For the exhaustive fixed-$B$ Stage~A test at a fixed node, the
$+1$ Monte Carlo feature p-values are superuniform under the complete null,
giving Bonferroni control of the nodewise rejection probability.
Empirically, adaptive stopping and histogram thresholding are the main
runtime controls. Disabling adaptive stopping makes CIF fits
4.0--8.4$\times$ slower by the median ratio across datasets, and exact threshold
search is 1.9--10.8$\times$ slower than histogram thresholding, with small
changes in downstream metrics and synthetic recovery. We then benchmark one
selected configuration per method family for 17 classification and 18 regression
feature selection methods on 23 classification and 8 regression datasets, including permutation test filters,
wrappers, single trees, forests, and gradient boosted models. In the main
real-data table, CIF ranks 4th of 17 on the 22 classification datasets where the
ctree and cforest references were run and 3rd of 18 on the smaller 8-dataset
regression benchmark. It also improves over the classical conditional inference tree and
forest references in matched comparisons. Classification diagnostics show CIF's
lowest mean ranks at the larger displayed values of $k$, while controlled
sparse high-$p$ simulations show that forest candidate sampling can exclude
informative features from many node-level tests. Overall, CIF has the strongest
empirical support among the conditional-inference feature selection methods
studied here. Adaptive stopping and histogram thresholding control its main
runtime costs, while the high-$p$ simulations identify candidate feature
availability as a remaining forest limitation.
