Conditional inference trees and forests separate feature selection from
threshold optimization. We make that separation explicit and study its
fixed-node feature-selection core. For a pre-specified node, a tested feature family, and
permutation budget $B$, the $+1$ Monte Carlo Stage~A p-value is super-uniform
under the complete null, so Bonferroni controls the Stage~A rejection
probability at that node. The practical question is then which expensive parts
of conditional-inference trees and forests can be approximated without materially
changing ranking behavior. In matched within-CIF ablations, adaptive stopping
provides 5--12$\times$ speedup with near-zero score loss, and histogram
thresholding provides another 1.9--10.7$\times$ speedup. We evaluate CIF in a
benchmark of 23 classification and 8 regression
datasets, with one selected configuration per method family and task. Empirically, CIF is the strongest conditional-inference method
in the benchmark, clearly improves on the historical conditional-inference
baselines, and remains competitive with strong generic ensembles in the real-data
benchmark. On the primary 14-dataset fixed-panel classification benchmark,
CIF outperforms \texttt{ctree}, \texttt{cforest}, and CIT on nearly every dataset;
the broader classification aggregate and the regression benchmark point
in the same direction. High-$p$, synthetic, and fixed-design diagnostics show
where the method weakens: in classification, CIF often improves beyond tiny
feature budgets without usually needing the full feature set, is better at
building useful top-$k$ sets than at identifying the exact top-ranked feature, and weakens
when forest subsampling leaves informative features underexposed.
