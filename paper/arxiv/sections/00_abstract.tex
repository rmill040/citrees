Conditional inference trees (CIT) and conditional inference forests (CIF)
reduce split-selection bias by testing candidate features before choosing split
thresholds, but repeated permutation tests and threshold searches can make these
methods computationally expensive. We study CIT and CIF as top-$k$
feature-ranking methods for downstream prediction using real-data benchmarks,
runtime ablations, and synthetic feature-recovery experiments.
At a fixed node, if the features and permutation budget do not depend on the
node responses, Bonferroni-corrected $+1$ Monte Carlo permutation $p$-values
control nodewise rejection under the complete permutation null. We then evaluate
CIT and CIF rankings on downstream prediction benchmarks.
In the real-data benchmark, CIF ranks 4th among 17 classification methods on 22
datasets and 3rd among 18 regression methods on 8 datasets; the regression
analyses use fewer datasets and have wider intervals.
With Bonferroni correction held fixed, the CIF runtime ablations identify two
runtime choices that matter most: adaptive stopping and threshold-search
granularity. Disabling adaptive stopping makes median runtime
4.0--8.4$\times$ larger across dataset groups, and exact threshold search is
1.9--10.8$\times$ slower than bounded histogram search, with small downstream
score changes. Sparse high-$p$
diagnostics show a separate issue: candidate feature sampling can leave
informative variables unavailable at many forest split decisions. Overall,
the results support CIF as a practical top-$k$ feature-ranking method in the
evaluated downstream prediction benchmarks.
