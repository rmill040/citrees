Conditional inference trees decouple feature screening from threshold
optimization through permutation testing, reducing the high-cardinality bias
of greedy CART-style split search. This paper is organized around two
questions that are often blurred together: which part of the procedure admits
finite-sample calibration, and how the full tree or forest should be evaluated
as a feature-ranking system.

Our theoretical analysis is intentionally narrow. In fixed-$B$ mode at a
pre-specified node, the $+1$ Monte Carlo permutation p-value used in Stage~A
is super-uniform under the complete permutation null, and Bonferroni controls
the probability of any Stage~A rejection at that node. These guarantees do not
automatically extend to adaptive internal nodes, Stage~B threshold searches,
or early-stopped permutation outputs.

The empirical sections are structured around a rank-then-evaluate benchmark on
synthetic and real tabular datasets, with confirmatory claims tied to
pre-specified primary endpoints and broader summaries treated descriptively.
The main practical theme is a strictness--depth tradeoff: conservative
nodewise screening improves calibration but can reduce the depth by which a
forest discovers weaker signals. The manuscript is organized to keep that
tradeoff, and the boundary between calibrated and adaptive quantities, easy to
read.
