Greedy decision trees select split variables by optimizing impurity over many
candidate thresholds, creating a multiple-comparisons effect: under a complete
null, high-cardinality features are more likely to appear informative by chance.
Conditional inference trees address this by separating variable screening from
split-point optimization via permutation-based hypothesis testing.

We study conditional inference trees and forests at two levels. First, we analyze
a fixed-$B$ reference regime that the implementation can run: a fixed node,
label-independent tested family, exhaustive Stage~A screening, and conservative
+1 Monte Carlo permutation p-values. In this regime, the Stage~A per-feature
p-values are super-uniform under a nodewise complete permutation null, and
Bonferroni yields complete-null bounds on the probability of any Stage~A
rejection at the fixed node and on the probability that the node accepts a
split on any particular tested feature, without dependence on the number of
candidate thresholds. We also show, via an idealized
exchangeable-score symmetry model for CART threshold search, that greedy CART
selection favors features in proportion to their candidate split count.

Second, we document the practical engineering optimizations used in the software,
including adaptive early stopping, candidate scanning, feature muting, threshold
subsampling, and forest resampling. These optimizations are important for speed
and scalability, but they are treated as algorithmic heuristics or empirical
design choices rather than as part of the fixed-$B$ theorem chain. We evaluate
feature rankings via a two-stage rank-then-evaluate benchmark on synthetic
ground-truth and real tabular datasets. A reference implementation is publicly
available.
