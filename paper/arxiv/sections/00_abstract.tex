Conditional inference trees and forests separate feature selection from
threshold optimization. We make that separation explicit and study its
fixed-node Stage~A setting. For a pre-specified node and permutation budget
$B$, the $+1$ Monte Carlo Stage~A p-value is super-uniform under the complete
null, so Bonferroni controls the Stage~A rejection probability at that node.
This guarantee applies only to fixed-node Stage~A, not to the full adaptive
learner. The practical question is which expensive parts
of conditional-inference trees and forests can be approximated without materially
changing feature-ranking performance. In matched ablations within conditional inference
forests (CIF), most runtime
savings come from adaptive stopping, which provides a 5.1--11.7$\times$
speedup with near-zero score change; histogram thresholding adds a separate
1.9--10.6$\times$ speedup relative to exact threshold search. We evaluate CIF
on 23 classification and 8 regression datasets, using one chosen setting for
each method and task. On the full classification benchmark, CIF performs best
among the
conditional-inference methods and remains in the range of several strong
generic ensembles. The 14-dataset
classification benchmark points in the same direction: CIF has a higher score
than \texttt{ctree} on 14/14 datasets, than \texttt{cforest} on 13/14, and
than conditional inference trees (CIT) on 13/14. Regression evidence points in
the same direction but is limited. High-$p$ results, synthetic datasets, and
controlled simulations show
where the method weakens: scores often peak at intermediate feature budgets,
the selected top-$k$ sets are better than the single top-ranked feature, and
sparse forests weaken when informative features are screened too rarely.
