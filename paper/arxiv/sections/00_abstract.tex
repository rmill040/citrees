Conditional inference trees and forests separate feature selection from
threshold optimization. We make that separation explicit and study its
fixed-node feature-selection core. For a pre-specified node, a tested feature family, and
permutation budget $B$, the $+1$ Monte Carlo Stage~A p-value is super-uniform
under the complete null, so Bonferroni controls the Stage~A rejection
probability at that node. This guarantee is local to fixed-node Stage~A rather
than end-to-end calibration for the full adaptive learner. The practical question is which expensive parts
of conditional-inference trees and forests can be approximated without materially
changing ranking behavior. In matched ablations within CIF, most runtime
savings come from adaptive stopping, which provides a 5.1--11.7$\times$
speedup with near-zero score change; histogram thresholding adds a separate
1.9--10.6$\times$ speedup relative to exact threshold search. We evaluate CIF on 23 classification and 8 regression
datasets, using one selected configuration per method family and task. On the
broader classification benchmark, CIF performs best among the
conditional-inference methods and remains in the range of several strong
generic ensembles. The 14-dataset
classification benchmark points in the same direction: CIF has a higher score
than \texttt{ctree} on 14/14 datasets, than \texttt{cforest} on 13/14, and
than CIT on 13/14. Regression evidence points in the same direction but is
limited. High-$p$, synthetic, and fixed-design diagnostics show
where the method weakens: scores often peak at intermediate feature budgets,
subset quality exceeds exact top-1 recovery, and sparse forests weaken when
informative features are screened too rarely.
