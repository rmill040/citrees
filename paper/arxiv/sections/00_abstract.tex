Conditional inference trees and forests separate feature selection from
threshold optimization. We make that separation explicit and study its
fixed-node feature-selection core. For a pre-specified node, a tested feature family, and
permutation budget $B$, the $+1$ Monte Carlo Stage~A p-value is super-uniform
under the complete null, so Bonferroni controls the Stage~A rejection
probability at that node. The practical question is then which expensive parts
of conditional-inference trees and forests can be approximated without materially
changing ranking behavior. In matched ablations within CIF, adaptive stopping
provides a 5.1--11.7$\times$ speedup with near-zero score change, and histogram
thresholding provides a separate 1.9--10.6$\times$ speedup relative to exact
threshold search. We evaluate CIF in a
benchmark of 23 classification and 8 regression
datasets, with one selected configuration per method family and task. On the
broader classification benchmark, CIF is the strongest conditional-inference
method, clearly improves on the historical conditional-inference baselines, and
is competitive with several strong generic ensembles on the classification
benchmark. A stricter 14-dataset
fixed-panel sensitivity check points in the same direction: CIF has a higher
score than \texttt{ctree} on 14/14 datasets, than \texttt{cforest} on 13/14,
and than CIT on 13/14. The smaller regression benchmark is supportive but more
limited. High-$p$, synthetic, and fixed-design diagnostics show
where the method weakens: in classification, CIF often improves beyond tiny
feature budgets without usually needing the full feature set, is better at
building useful top-$k$ sets than at identifying the exact top-ranked feature, and weakens
when forest subsampling leaves informative features out of the candidate set too often.
