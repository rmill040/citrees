Conditional inference trees (CIT) and conditional inference forests (CIF)
reduce split selection bias by testing candidate split features before choosing
split thresholds. That separation is relevant for feature selection because
rankings from fitted trees and forests can otherwise reflect each feature's
number of possible split thresholds. Repeated permutation tests and threshold
searches, however, make the approach computationally expensive. We study
whether CIT and CIF can be used as practical feature selection methods by
measuring how runtime hyperparameters affect feature rankings,
downstream prediction, and feature recovery on synthetic datasets with known
informative features. For exhaustive fixed-$B$ Stage~A testing at a fixed node,
when the tested feature set and resample budget are label-independent, $+1$
Monte Carlo $p$-values give Bonferroni control of nodewise rejection under the
complete nodewise permutation null. In CIF,
adaptive stopping and histogram thresholding account for the largest measured
runtime changes: without adaptive stopping, CIF training is 4.0--8.4$\times$
slower across datasets, and exact threshold search is 1.9--10.8$\times$ slower
than histogram thresholding. The corresponding absolute changes are at most
0.011 in downstream prediction metrics and 0.017 in synthetic feature recovery.
In the real data benchmark, with one selected configuration per method,
CIF ranks 4th of 17 classification methods on 22
datasets and 3rd of 18 methods on a smaller 8-dataset regression benchmark.
In direct classification comparisons with the original conditional inference tree
and forest implementations, CIF has higher average downstream scores on 22/22
and 19/22 datasets, respectively. The regression point estimates have the same
direction, with CIF higher on 7/8 datasets against ctree and cforest and on 6/8
against CIT, but the regression benchmark is smaller and the uncertainty
intervals are wider. CIF's lowest
classification mean ranks occur at larger values of $k$, and its lowest
displayed regression mean rank occurs at $k=50$. Sparse high-$p$ simulations
indicate that candidate feature sampling can leave informative features out of
many local split tests.
Overall, these experiments support CIF mainly as a predictive top-$k$ ranking
method; exact recovery of the first informative feature is less consistent,
especially in the synthetic recovery checks.
