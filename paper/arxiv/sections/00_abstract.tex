Conditional inference trees (CIT) and conditional inference forests (CIF)
reduce split selection bias by testing candidate split features before choosing
split thresholds, but repeated permutation tests and threshold searches make
that separation computationally expensive. We study CIT and CIF as feature
selection methods and ask which computations can be reduced without reducing
ranking quality. For the exhaustive fixed-$B$ Stage~A test at a fixed node, the
$+1$ Monte Carlo feature p-values are superuniform under the complete null,
giving Bonferroni control of the nodewise rejection probability.
Empirically, adaptive stopping and bounded threshold search are the main
runtime controls. Disabling adaptive stopping makes CIF fits
4.0--8.4$\times$ slower by the median ratio across datasets, and exact threshold
search is 1.9--10.8$\times$ slower than histogram thresholding, with small
changes in downstream score and synthetic recovery. We then benchmark 17
classification and 18 regression feature selection methods on 23 classification
and 8 regression datasets, including permutation test filters, wrappers, single
trees, forests, and gradient boosted models. CIF ranks
4th of 17 on classification and 3rd of 18 on regression, and improves over
classical conditional inference tree and forest references in matched
comparisons. Additional analyses show that CIF often becomes more competitive
as downstream models use larger selected feature sets, while controlled sparse
high-$p$ simulations show that candidate sampling in forests can hide
informative features before testing sees them. Overall, conditional inference
can be used as a practical feature selection strategy: the split selection
rationale is retained, and the dominant runtime costs are controlled without
large changes in downstream benchmark scores or synthetic recovery.
