\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Feature selection in tabular learning has two jobs: improve downstream
prediction by removing noise variables, and produce a ranking that users can
inspect and compare across datasets. Tree-based methods are attractive for
both because they capture nonlinearity and interactions while yielding an
embedded notion of variable importance. The statistical difficulty is that
split selection is itself an adaptive search: a chosen variable wins many
data-dependent comparisons, and later nodes depend on earlier
label-dependent splits.

That difficulty appears already at the root. CART-style trees optimize an
impurity criterion over the union of all variable-threshold pairs
\citep{Breiman1984ClassificationAndRegressionTrees}. Features with many
admissible cutpoints therefore have more opportunities to attain an extreme
score by chance, which induces the well-known high-cardinality bias in tree
induction and random-forest importance measures
\citep{LohShih1997SplitSelectionMethods,KimLoh2001ClassificationTreesUnbiasedMultiwaySplits,Loh2002RegressionTreesUnbiasedVariableSelectionInteractionDetection,Strobl2007BiasRFVariableImportance}.

Conditional inference trees address that bias by separating \emph{which
variable} to split from \emph{where} to split it
\citep{Hothorn2006UnbiasedRecursivePartitioning}. Stage~A screens candidate
features with hypothesis tests; Stage~B then optimizes the threshold within
the selected feature. That separation gives a clean fixed-node screening
object to which classical permutation arguments apply. The same Stage~A
machinery also yields an embedded feature ranker when it is used inside a tree
or forest.

The fixed-node screening object, however, is not the whole learner. A modern
implementation also needs early stopping, candidate ordering, threshold
subsampling, feature muting, and forest resampling to stay computationally
usable. Those choices are often sensible, but they change the inferential
object. The organizing principle of this paper is therefore simple: we
separate the \emph{calibrated screening claims} that hold for fixed-node
Stage~A in fixed-$B$ mode from the \emph{engineering behavior} of the full
adaptive tree or forest.

Our scope is deliberately narrower than general post-selection inference for
recursive trees, and narrower than global FDR/FWER control for end-to-end
feature-selection workflows. Those are important but different targets
\citep{Berk2013ValidPostSelectionInference,Lee2016ExactPostSelectionInferenceLasso,LeebPotscher2015ConfidenceIntervalsAfterModelSelection,MeinshausenBuhlmann2010StabilitySelection,BarberCandes2015Knockoffs}.
Here we focus on fixed-node Stage~A screening under the complete permutation
null, and on the empirical consequences of embedding that screening rule
inside a practical feature-ranking system.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
First, we give a clean statement of what is and is not calibrated in a
conditional-inference workflow: fixed-node Stage~A p-values in the fixed-$B$
reference regime admit classical finite-sample permutation guarantees,
whereas Stage~B threshold searches, internal-node quantities, and early-stopped
outputs are treated as adaptive statistics. Second, we present an
implementation-oriented conditional-inference tree and forest library that
adds modern runtime optimizations while keeping the reference regime explicit.
Third, we evaluate the method as a feature ranker on synthetic and real
tabular datasets and show that its main practical bottleneck is conservative
nodewise screening: Bonferroni correction suppresses tree depth, which in turn
limits the forest's ability to recover weaker signals in high-dimensional
settings.

\Cref{sec:scope} frames the split-selection problem and the inferential target
