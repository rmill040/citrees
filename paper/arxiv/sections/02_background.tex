\section{Background and related work}
\label{sec:background}

\paragraph{Greedy trees and selection bias.}
Classical CART-style decision trees choose a split variable and split point by
optimizing an impurity objective over a large set of candidates
\citep{Breiman1984ClassificationAndRegressionTrees}. For a continuous feature
with many unique values, the candidate set of midpoints can be large; when the
tree compares the best candidate across many features, the procedure resembles
an extreme-value search. Under global null models, this creates a
multiple-comparisons effect: features with more candidate thresholds are more
likely to look best by chance. This mechanism has been analyzed in the context
of random-forest importance measures \citep{Strobl2007BiasRFVariableImportance},
and motivates both test-based variable selection and alternative split-selection
procedures designed to reduce selection bias \citep{LohShih1997SplitSelectionMethods}.

\paragraph{Conditional inference trees and forests.}
Conditional inference trees were introduced by
\citet{Hothorn2006UnbiasedRecursivePartitioning} as an alternative to greedy
impurity optimization. At a node, the conditional inference procedure first
tests each feature for association with the response via permutation testing
(variable screening) and only then searches for a split point (threshold
selection). This separation is designed to reduce selection bias from differing
numbers of candidate split points. The framework is widely used via available
implementations \citep{Hothorn2006UnbiasedRecursivePartitioning}.
The permutation-statistics foundation for these linear-statistic tests is
developed by \citet{StrasserWeber1999PermutationStatistics}.
In this paper we focus on the inferentially clean part of the framework: fixed-node
(pre-specified; especially root) feature screening with Monte Carlo permutation p-values.

\paragraph{Tree ensembles and scalable split search.}
Random forests \citep{Breiman2001RandomForests} and extremely randomized trees
\citep{Geurts2006ExtraTrees} add algorithmic randomness (row bootstrap,
feature subsampling, and/or randomized threshold proposals) to reduce variance
and improve scalability. Random forest theory and consistency have also been
studied in depth; see, e.g., \citep{Scornet2015ConsistencyRandomForests} and the
survey \citep{BiauScornet2016RandomForestGuidedTour}.
Modern gradient-boosting systems
\citep{Friedman2001GBM,ChenGuestrin2016XGBoost,Ke2017LightGBM,Prokhorenkova2018CatBoost}
further emphasize computational efficiency, often using approximate split
search (e.g., histogram-based threshold grids).
Bayesian tree ensembles such as BART provide a complementary nonparametric
alternative \citep{Chipman2010BART}.
Extensions of tree ensembles to other outcomes include random survival forests
for right-censored data \citep{Ishwaran2008RandomSurvivalForests}.

\paragraph{Feature selection baselines.}
The feature-selection literature includes \emph{filter} methods based on
univariate dependence scores (e.g., correlation, mutual information, distance
correlation \citep{Kraskov2004EstimatingMutualInformation,Szekely2007DistanceCorrelation},
and randomized dependence coefficients \citep{LopezPaz2013RDC}), as well as
redundancy-aware filters such as mRMR \citep{Peng2005MRMR}. It also includes
\emph{wrapper} and \emph{embedded} approaches that use model refits to assess
importance, such as recursive feature elimination \citep{Guyon2002RFE},
Boruta-style all-relevant selection \citep{Kursa2010Boruta}, permutation
importance \citep{Breiman2001RandomForests,Strobl2008ConditionalVIM}, and
SHAP-based attributions \citep{Lundberg2017SHAP}. In our experiments, we
evaluate methods primarily as rankers using a two-stage rank-then-evaluate
protocol rather than focusing only on in-sample impurity objectives.

\paragraph{Permutation testing and Monte Carlo p-values.}
Permutation tests provide a nonparametric route to calibration under an
exchangeability assumption (invariance of the joint label distribution to
permutations, conditional on covariates treated as fixed by the permutation
scheme). In Monte Carlo permutation testing, the
Phipson--Smyth ``+1'' correction yields p-values that are finite-sample
super-uniform (conservative under the null) under exchangeability \citep{PhipsonSmyth2010PermutationPValues}.
There is also a large literature on sequential Monte Carlo tests and optional
stopping \citep{BesagClifford1991SequentialMonteCarloPValues,Gandy2009SequentialImplementationMonteCarloTests};
we treat early stopping as a computational heuristic and do not make
optional-stopping validity claims for early-stopped outputs in this paper.
