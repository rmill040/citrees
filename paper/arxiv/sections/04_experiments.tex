\section{Experimental design}
\label{sec:experiments}

Every method is treated as a feature ranker: it produces an ordering on the
training split, and downstream predictors are evaluated on the top-$k$ features.

\subsection{Benchmark protocol}
\label{sec:experiments-protocol}

All real-data comparisons use 5-fold cross-validation over 5 random seeds. The
standard budgets are $k \in \{5,10,25,50,100\}$. Datasets with $p>100$ also
evaluate larger budgets up to $k=p$, but those endpoint budgets are used only
for the separate high-$p$ saturation check. The classification benchmark
compares 15 methods and the regression benchmark 16. Classification uses
balanced accuracy with logistic regression, SVM, and KNN as downstream models;
regression uses $R^2$ with ridge, SVR, and KNN.

\subsection{Primary endpoint and sensitivity checks}
\label{sec:experiments-analysis-plan}

The main comparison is the fixed-panel classification benchmark, which holds
dataset membership constant across downstream models and budgets. The broader
classification benchmark over changing feature budgets is secondary because dataset support
shrinks as $k$ grows. The high-$p$ analysis asks whether CIF improves once
$k$ exceeds 100, and the synthetic analyses ask how well the ranking
matches known signal rather than downstream prediction.

Dataset support shrinks as $k$ grows: classification retains
$22, 21, 15, 15, 14$ datasets at $k = 5, 10, 25, 50, 100$; regression retains
$8, 8, 7, 7, 6$.

Within each $(\text{dataset}, \text{downstream model}, k)$ cell, scores are
first averaged across folds and seeds. Cross-method summaries then rank methods
within each cell and average those ranks over the supported cells. The fixed
panel also reports paired CIF-versus-baseline intervals and a Friedman omnibus
test. The fixed classification panel retains 14 of the 23 datasets. The
benchmark tables report the support counts.

\subsection{Metric definitions}
\label{sec:experiments-metrics}

Several metrics recur in the results. The \emph{share of informative features selected} is the
fraction of informative features in the top-$k$ set. \emph{Top-1 hit rate} is
the fraction of synthetic datasets where the first ranked feature is informative.
\emph{Informative split share} is the fraction of a tree's splits that use
informative features. \emph{First budget that reaches the best score} is the smallest $k$ at
which a method reaches its peak downstream score. \emph{Kendall's $W$} is the
Friedman-test effect size.

\subsection{Datasets and preprocessing}
\label{sec:experiments-datasets}

The packaged real-data benchmark contains 23 classification datasets and 8
regression datasets under \texttt{paper/data/}. Classification is the primary
benchmark because 23 datasets support a stronger cross-method comparison than 8.
The high-$p$ saturation check restricts attention to the 15 classification and
6 regression datasets with more than 100 features.

The synthetic benchmark contains 8 classification and 8 regression datasets
with known informative features and controlled nuisance structure. A separate
mechanism study fixes $n=250$ and varies
$p \in \{100, 500, 1000\}$ and
$n_{\mathrm{informative}} \in \{1,2,5,10\}$. Tree methods include CIT, DT, and
RT; forest methods include CIF, CIF-all, RF, and ET with 1000 trees.

Features are standardized using training-fold statistics and the same transform
is applied to the held-out split.

\subsection{Compared methods}
\label{sec:experiments-methods}

The benchmark compares three method families. Permutation-calibrated filters
rank features from univariate dependence scores: MC and RDC for classification,
and PC, DC, and RDC for regression. Embedded methods derive rankings from tree
or ensemble importance scores: CIT, CIF, RF, ExtraTrees, XGBoost, LightGBM,
CatBoost, and the historical \texttt{partykit}/\texttt{party} references
\texttt{ctree} and \texttt{cforest} \citep{HothornZeileis2015Partykit}. Wrapper
baselines use a downstream estimator to score subsets: Boruta, PI, CPI
\citep{DebeerStrobl2020ConditionalPermutationImportanceRevisited}, and RFE
\citep{Guyon2002RFE}.

\subsection{Config selection and implementation defaults}
\label{sec:experiments-repro}

Several families, including CIT, CIF, and the permutation-test filters, are
evaluated under multiple configurations. For each method family and task, the benchmark
selects the configuration with the highest mean score across downstream models
and the standard budget set $\{5,10,25,50,100\}$. The reported benchmark ranks
therefore summarize the tuned surface. They are not tuning-free external
estimates. The leave-one-dataset-out analysis on the fixed
classification panel checks that CIF's placement is not driven by a single
dataset. It is still not external validation.

For the conditional-inference methods, the benchmark uses the following
defaults: adaptive sequential stopping as the main runtime control, Bonferroni
correction for stricter selection, feature muting, feature and threshold
ordering for early stopping, and histogram thresholding with
256 bins as the threshold-search approximation. Selector family, honesty, forest
resampling, and tree-versus-forest structure vary across the selected CIT/CIF
configurations (justified by the ablation analysis in \cref{sec:results}). The
dedicated within-CIF timing studies then perturb that package one control at a
time: adaptive stopping, Bonferroni correction, feature scanning, threshold
scanning, muting, bootstrap, and threshold-search budgets.
