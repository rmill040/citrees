\section{Experimental design}
\label{sec:experiments}

Each method produces an ordering on the training split, and downstream
predictors are evaluated on the top-$k$ features.

\subsection{Benchmark protocol}
\label{sec:experiments-protocol}

All real-data comparisons use 5-fold cross-validation over 5 random seeds. The
standard budgets are $k \in \{5,10,25,50,100\}$. Datasets with $p>100$ also
evaluate larger budgets up to $k=p$, but those endpoint budgets are used only
for the separate high-$p$ saturation check. The classification benchmark
includes 15 methods; the regression benchmark includes 16. Classification uses
balanced accuracy with logistic regression, SVM, and KNN as downstream models;
regression uses $R^2$ with ridge, SVR, and KNN. Within each task, the paper
reports one selected configuration per method family on the shared benchmark
surface, so the paper compares method families rather than multiple tuned
variants from the same family.

\subsection{Primary endpoint and sensitivity checks}
\label{sec:experiments-analysis-plan}

The main real-data comparison is the broader classification benchmark over the
standard feature budgets. Because support shrinks as $k$ grows, we also report
a 14-dataset classification benchmark with complete coverage across downstream
models and standard feature budgets. The high-$p$ analysis asks
whether CIF benefits beyond $k{=}100$ and whether it usually needs all features.
The synthetic analyses measure how well the ranking tracks
known signal rather than downstream prediction.

Dataset support shrinks as $k$ grows: classification includes
$22, 21, 15, 15, 14$ datasets at $k = 5, 10, 25, 50, 100$; regression includes
$8, 8, 7, 7, 6$.

Within each $(\text{dataset}, \text{downstream model}, k)$ cell, scores are
averaged across folds and seeds. We then average those cell-level scores and
ranks within each dataset over the downstream models and budgets available for
that dataset. The benchmark tables average those dataset summaries over the
datasets included in each surface. The 14-dataset benchmark also reports paired
CIF-versus-baseline intervals and a Friedman omnibus summary on the same
dataset-mean surface. The 14-dataset classification benchmark includes 14 of
the 23 datasets. These are the classification datasets with complete support
across all downstream models and the standard feature budgets. As a separate
config-selection sensitivity check, we rerun the one-config-per-family
selection in leave-one-dataset-out form on this 14-dataset panel: for each
held-out dataset, configurations are selected on the other 13 datasets and
then evaluated on the held-out dataset. This sensitivity uses the same
cell-level metric and the same within-dataset averaging over downstream models
and standard feature budgets as the 14-dataset benchmark; only the
config-selection split changes. We summarize it by the mean held-out score and
mean held-out rank across the 14 datasets, along with how often the global
benchmark-selected configuration is reselected.

\subsection{Metric definitions}
\label{sec:experiments-metrics}

The following metrics recur in the results. The \emph{share of informative features selected} is the
fraction of informative features in the top-$k$ set. \emph{Top-1 hit rate} is
the fraction of synthetic datasets where the first ranked feature is informative.
\emph{Informative split share} is the fraction of a tree's splits that use
informative features. \emph{First budget that reaches the best score} is the smallest $k$
at which a method reaches its peak downstream score. \emph{Kendall's $W$} is the
Friedman-test effect size.

\subsection{Datasets and preprocessing}
\label{sec:experiments-datasets}

The real-data benchmark contains 23 classification datasets and 8
regression datasets. Classification is the primary
benchmark and also provides broader real-data coverage: 23 datasets rather
than 8.
The high-$p$ saturation check restricts attention to the 15 classification and
6 regression datasets with more than 100 features.

The synthetic benchmark contains 8 classification and 8 regression datasets
with known informative features and controlled noise structure. A separate
mechanism study uses $n=250$ and varies
$p \in \{100, 500, 1000\}$ and
$n_{\mathrm{informative}} \in \{1,2,5,10\}$. Tree methods include CIT, DT, and
RT; forest methods include CIF, CIF-all, RF, and ET with 1000 trees.

Features are standardized using training-fold statistics and the same transform
is applied to the held-out split.

\subsection{Compared methods}
\label{sec:experiments-methods}

The benchmark includes three method families. Permutation-calibrated filters
use univariate dependence scores to rank features: MC and RDC for classification,
and PC, DC, and RDC for regression, with RDC following
\citet{LopezPaz2013RDC} and distance correlation following
\citet{Szekely2007DistanceCorrelation}. Embedded methods use tree
or ensemble importance scores: CIT, CIF, RF \citep{Breiman2001RandomForests},
ExtraTrees \citep{Geurts2006ExtraTrees}, XGBoost
\citep{ChenGuestrin2016XGBoost}, LightGBM \citep{Ke2017LightGBM},
CatBoost \citep{Prokhorenkova2018CatBoost}, and the historical
\texttt{partykit}/\texttt{party} references \texttt{ctree} and
\texttt{cforest}
\citep{Hothorn2006UnbiasedRecursivePartitioning,HothornZeileis2015Partykit}.
Wrapper baselines use a downstream estimator to score feature subsets: Boruta
\citep{Kursa2010Boruta}, PI, CPI
\citep{DebeerStrobl2020ConditionalPermutationImportanceRevisited}, and RFE
\citep{Guyon2002RFE}.

\subsection{Config selection and implementation defaults}
\label{sec:experiments-repro}

Several families, including CIT, CIF, and the permutation-test filters, are
run under multiple configurations. For each method family and task, the benchmark
selects the configuration with the highest mean score across downstream models
and the standard budget set $\{5,10,25,50,100\}$. The benchmark summaries
therefore report one final configuration per method family within each task
across all datasets, downstream models, and feature budgets.
The synthetic ranking diagnostics use a separate per-family synthetic-best
configuration, chosen by informative-feature recovery over the standard
synthetic budgets.

For the conditional-inference methods, the benchmark uses the following
defaults: adaptive sequential stopping, Bonferroni correction, feature muting,
and feature and threshold scanning when early stopping is active. Histogram
thresholding with 256 bins is used for threshold search. The CIT/CIF
configurations in the benchmark differ in selector family, honesty, forest resampling,
and whether the method is a tree or forest. The CIF timing
studies change one setting at a time. They cover adaptive stopping,
Bonferroni correction, feature scanning, threshold scanning, muting,
bootstrap, and threshold-search budgets.
