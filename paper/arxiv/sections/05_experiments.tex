\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

This section defines our benchmark protocol and analysis-plan contract for
synthetic ground-truth datasets and real tabular datasets. The empirical goal is
to evaluate methods as \emph{feature rankers} in a way that separates ranking
quality from downstream model choice. Until the results tables/figures are
populated, this section should be read as a pre-results specification rather than
as evidence for method superiority.

\paragraph{Two empirical roles.}
The experiments play two different roles in the paper.
First, we run calibration and selection-bias diagnostics for quantities that
are directly tied to the fixed-node theory, namely Stage~A screening in fixed-$B$
mode under complete-null designs.
Second, we evaluate the full tree/forest implementation in its practical
engineering modes, where adaptive stopping and related heuristics are judged by
ranking quality and runtime rather than by theorem-backed p-value validity.

\subsection{Protocol: rank-then-evaluate}
\label{sec:experiments-protocol}

We treat each method as a \emph{ranker} and use a two-stage protocol:
(i)~produce a feature ranking on a training split, and
(ii)~train downstream models on the top-$k$ selected features and evaluate on
held-out data. This protocol isolates ranking quality from downstream model
choice and avoids leakage by fitting all preprocessing on the training portion
only. We use 5-fold cross-validation repeated over 5 random seeds and evaluate
$k \in \{5,10,25,50,100,p\}$ (clipped to $k \le p$). Details and notation are in
\cref{app:methods-benchmark}.

\subsection{Primary endpoints and analysis plan (pre-specified)}
\label{sec:experiments-analysis-plan}

Because the protocol produces results across multiple $k$ values, downstream
models, and metrics, we pre-specify primary endpoints to reduce degrees of
freedom in the headline comparisons.

\paragraph{Primary endpoints (real datasets).}
For classification, the primary endpoint is balanced accuracy of logistic
regression trained on the top-$k$ features with $k=25$. For regression, the
primary endpoint is $R^2$ of ridge regression trained on the top-$k$ features
with $k=25$. All other $k$ values, downstream models, and metrics are treated as
secondary and reported as robustness checks.

\paragraph{Aggregation.}
For the pre-specified headline endpoints above, dataset-level summaries are
formed by averaging across folds and random seeds after fixing the relevant
downstream model and $k$. The current generic analysis scripts also emit
broader diagnostic summaries at the \texttt{method\_base} family level; those
pipeline-wide summaries average within dataset across locked configuration
variants and, in their all-model form, also across downstream models and $k$
values before cross-dataset comparison. The current scripts produce average-rank
tables, Friedman omnibus tests, Holm-corrected paired Wilcoxon comparisons,
critical-difference diagrams, and bootstrap-CI summary tables. In the final
paper, headline empirical claims will be restricted to the pre-specified
primary endpoints above, with broader all-model, per-model, and per-$k$
analyses reported as robustness diagnostics.

\paragraph{Synthetic ground truth.}
For synthetic datasets with known informative feature sets, the primary ranking
metrics are precision@k, recall@k, and F1@k at
$k\in\{5,10,20,|S^\star|\}$ (with $k$ clipped to $k\le p$). In dedicated
selection-bias analyses on null-style regimes with known noise features, we
also examine noise-selection diagnostics. These definitions match the
current analysis script configuration in \cref{app:methods-benchmark}.

\subsection{Datasets and preprocessing}
\label{sec:experiments-datasets}

All datasets are stored as Parquet files under \texttt{paper/data/}.

\paragraph{Synthetic ground truth.}
We use curated synthetic suites with known informative feature sets:
8 classification datasets (\texttt{paper/data/classification/synthetic/}) and
8 regression datasets (\texttt{paper/data/regression/synthetic/}). The suites
include regimes designed to probe selection bias (e.g., high-cardinality noise),
nonlinear dependence, correlated feature blocks, redundancy, high-dimensional
scaling, and weak-signal settings.

\paragraph{Real datasets.}
We evaluate on 23 real classification datasets
(\path{paper/data/classification/real/}) and 8 real regression datasets
(\path{paper/data/regression/real/}), emphasizing tabular feature selection
regimes including $p \gg n$.

\paragraph{Preprocessing and leakage prevention.}
All preprocessing is fit on the training portion only within each fold and then
applied to the held-out portion. We standardize features using training-fold
means and variances; see \cref{app:methods-benchmark} for the code-aligned
definition. A dataset summary table (provenance and $n/p$ statistics) will be
added in the finalized version.

\subsection{Methods and fairness policy}
\label{sec:experiments-methods}

The current executable benchmark roster compares three families of feature
rankers.
First, \emph{permutation-calibrated univariate selectors} convert dependence
scores into permutation p-values (currently
\texttt{ptest\_mc}, \texttt{ptest\_rdc}, \texttt{ptest\_pc}, and
\texttt{ptest\_dc}).
Second, \emph{embedded} selectors from tree and ensemble learners (including
\emph{conditional-inference} trees/forests
\citep{Hothorn2006UnbiasedRecursivePartitioning,HothornBuehlmannDudoitMolinaroVanDerLaan2006SurvivalEnsembles,HothornZeileis2015Partykit}
and common CART-style baselines
\citep{Breiman1984ClassificationAndRegressionTrees,Breiman2001RandomForests,Geurts2006ExtraTrees,Pedregosa2011ScikitLearn}); and
third, \emph{wrappers} such as Boruta, RF-based permutation-importance
baselines, an approximate conditional-permutation variant, and RF-based RFE
\citep{Kursa2010Boruta,Guyon2002RFE,DebeerStrobl2020ConditionalPermutationImportanceRevisited}.
The broader feature-selection literature also includes raw filters such as
mutual information and mRMR, but those are background comparators rather than
part of the current locked benchmark grid. Implementation-aligned definitions
of the selector and splitter statistics used in our experiments are in
\cref{app:methods-selectors,app:methods-splitters}.

\paragraph{Hyperparameters and compute budgets.}
To avoid optimistic bias from unequal tuning effort, each baseline is evaluated
under a pre-specified hyperparameter grid and compute budget. In the current
analysis pipeline, confirmatory cross-dataset summaries are reported at the
method-family level rather than the config-hash level: multiple locked
configurations for the same base method are averaged within dataset before
cross-dataset comparison. The final version will include the full method
roster, grids, and failure/timeout policy aligned to the experiment pipeline
configuration.

\subsection{Results}
\label{sec:experiments-results}

We first validate the statistical machinery (calibration, selection bias), then
evaluate ranking quality on synthetic and real datasets, and finally report
ablation experiments isolating the contribution of each design choice.

% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Selection bias under a complete global null}
\label{sec:experiments-selection-bias}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figures/selection_bias_demo.png}
  \caption{Selection bias under a complete global null
  ($\alpha{=}0.05$, Bonferroni-adjusted $\alpha/m{=}0.0167$).
  Left: CART-style greedy split selection favors high-cardinality features
  (continuous selected ${\sim}60\%$, categorical-$50$ selected ${\sim}33\%$).
  Right: conditional-inference Stage~A screening rejects all features
  ($\Pr(\text{split}){=}0.000$), correctly returning a stump.}
  \label{fig:selection-bias-demo}
\end{figure}

\Cref{fig:selection-bias-demo} demonstrates the selection-bias problem under a
complete global null ($X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y$) with three feature types:
continuous, binary, and categorical with 50 levels. CART-style greedy split
optimization selects the continuous feature at the root roughly 60\% of the
time, because more candidate thresholds yield more opportunities to find a
spuriously good split. The conditional-inference framework eliminates this bias
entirely: the Stage~A permutation test rejects all features at the
Bonferroni-adjusted level, producing a stump in every replication.

% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{P-value calibration under the null}
\label{sec:experiments-calibration}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figures/calibration_pvalue_ecdf.png}
  \caption{Empirical CDF (left) and histogram (right) of Stage~A $+1$
  permutation p-values under a complete global null
  ($n{=}200$, $B{=}199$, 10\,000 simulations). Both
  fixed-$B$ selectors (MC, PC) and the adaptive-stopping variant lie at or
  below the Uniform$(0,1)$ diagonal, confirming super-uniformity.}
  \label{fig:pvalue-calibration}
\end{figure}

Under a complete global null with $n{=}200$ observations and $B{=}199$
permutations, the $+1$ corrected MC p-value is super-uniform
(\cref{fig:pvalue-calibration}). \Cref{tab:calibration} reports empirical
rejection rates at $\alpha{=}0.05$ across permutation budgets and stopping
rules. The fixed-$B$ MC test achieves rejection rates ranging from 0.041
($B{=}49$) to 0.055 ($B{=}199$), all within the expected binomial fluctuation
band around the nominal 0.05 level. Adaptive early stopping is marginally
conservative: 0.047 at $B{=}199$, consistent with the Beta-CDF posterior
concentrating slightly below the frequentist p-value.

\begin{table}[t]
  \centering

  $\alpha{=}0.05$, across permutation budgets $B$ and stopping rules.}
  \label{tab:calibration}
  \small
  \begin{tabular}{llcc}
    \toprule
    Selector & Stopping & $B$ & Rejection rate \\
    \midrule
    MC & fixed-$B$ & 49  & 0.041 \\
    MC & fixed-$B$ & 99  & 0.046 \\
    MC & fixed-$B$ & 199 & 0.055 \\
    MC & fixed-$B$ & 499 & 0.050 \\
    MC & fixed-$B$ & 999 & 0.050 \\
    PC & fixed-$B$ & 199 & 0.049 \\
    MC & adaptive  & 199 & 0.047 \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The power analysis (\cref{fig:power}) shows that both stopping rules achieve
near-identical power under the alternative. At weak signal strength
(class separation $= 0.1$), rejection rate reaches 0.66--0.71 depending on $B$;
at moderate signal ($\geq 0.5$), power saturates at 1.0 for all
configurations. A noteworthy edge case: at $\alpha{=}0.01$ with $B{=}49$, the
fixed-$B$ test has zero power because the minimum achievable p-value
$(0{+}1)/(49{+}1) = 0.02$ exceeds $\alpha$. Adaptive stopping resolves this
resolution barrier by reporting a Bayesian posterior estimate.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.48\linewidth]{figures/ablation_power.png}
  \caption{Power curve: rejection rate vs.\ signal strength (class separation)
  at $\alpha{=}0.05$. Both adaptive and full (fixed-$B$) stopping rules
  maintain Type~I error at or below 0.05 under the null (class separation $=0$)
  and achieve identical power under the alternative.}
  \label{fig:power}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Synthetic ground-truth ranking quality}
\label{sec:experiments-synthetic}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/synthetic_cd_precision_at_10_clean.png}
  \caption{Critical-difference diagram for precision@10 on synthetic datasets
  with known informative features. Methods connected by a gray bar are not
  significantly different (Nemenyi test, $\alpha{=}0.05$, CD $= 6.85$).
  CIF achieves mean rank 7.0, within the top clique alongside CatBoost (5.0),
  RFE (5.7), and RF (6.3).}
  \label{fig:synthetic-ranking}
\end{figure}

On synthetic datasets with known informative feature sets,
\cref{fig:synthetic-ranking} shows the Nemenyi critical-difference diagram for
precision@10. CIF achieves mean rank 7.0, tied with the permutation-test
baselines (MC-ptest 6.3, PC-ptest 7.0, DC-ptest 7.0) and not significantly
different from the top-ranked CatBoost (5.0) or RFE (5.7). The R
implementations of conditional inference perform substantially worse:
R-cforest ranks 14.0 and R-ctree 17.0 (last), reflecting the performance gap
between our optimized implementation and the original R packages.

CIF's synthetic performance is regime-dependent: on Toeplitz-correlated
features, CIF achieves 0.90 precision@10, close to the best (ptest\_mc at
1.00). On weak-signal data (class separation $= 0.3$), CIF drops to 0.12--0.26
precision@10, below RF (0.14--0.16) and ExtraTrees (0.16), because the
permutation test correctly but aggressively rejects weak-but-real features. This
trade-off between Type~I error control and power is further analyzed in the
ablation (\cref{sec:experiments-ablation}).

\subsubsection{Runtime and scalability}
\label{sec:experiments-runtime}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.75\linewidth]{figures/ablation_scaling.png}
  \caption{Runtime scaling of CIF vs.\ RF and ExtraTrees. (a)~Varying
  $n$ at $p{=}100$: CIF scales roughly linearly in $n$.
  (b)~Varying $p$ at $n{=}1000$: CIF runtime is nearly independent of $p$
  thanks to feature muting and adaptive stopping.}
  \label{fig:runtime}
\end{figure}

Permutation testing adds a fixed computational overhead per dataset.
At typical feature-selection scales ($n{=}1000$, $p{=}100$), CIF completes in
${\sim}34$\,s---practical for the offline, one-time nature of feature selection.
At larger sample sizes ($n{=}5000$), runtime grows to ${\sim}143$\,s, scaling
roughly linearly in $n$ (\cref{fig:runtime}a). By comparison, RF and ET
complete in under 0.25\,s at all tested scales; the gap reflects the
permutation-test overhead rather than an algorithmic inefficiency in the
tree-building itself. Adaptive early stopping reduces this cost by approximately $2.5\times$
compared to full permutation tests (e.g., 55\,s vs.\ 141\,s on the easy
dataset with $\alpha{=}0.20$).

CIF's runtime is remarkably insensitive to the number of features
(\cref{fig:runtime}b). Across a $50\times$ range from $p{=}20$ to $p{=}1000$
at $n{=}1000$, CIF runtime stays within 29--35\,s. Feature muting and adaptive
stopping prune uninformative features quickly, so the per-feature marginal cost
is near zero once the informative features have been identified.

\paragraph{CIF vs.\ R implementations.}
The R implementations rank significantly below CIF (R-ctree at 14.9,
R-cforest at 14.0) despite using the same underlying conditional-inference
framework. CIF wins 22 of 23 real classification datasets against both R-ctree
and R-cforest, with a mean balanced-accuracy gap of $+0.09$. We investigated
whether this gap reflects an unfair comparison: the R configs in our grid
include both Bonferroni and MonteCarlo test types, matching the ablation
settings where R-ctree achieves strong precision on full data.
The key difference is that R-ctree's strict Bonferroni test is fragile to sample
reduction: under 5-fold cross-validation (80\% training data), the reduced
sample size makes the permutation test too conservative, collapsing trees to
near-empty rankings. CIF avoids this through feature muting, scanning, and
adaptive stopping, which compensate for the sample-size reduction. This
robustness to subsampling is itself a practical contribution of the engineering
optimizations.

\paragraph{Performance at varying $k$.}
CIF's ranking quality improves with the number of selected features. At
$k{=}5$, CIF ranks 6.8; by $k{=}50$, CIF narrows the gap to RF (5.9 vs.\
5.9). At $k{=}100$, CIF (6.3) overtakes RF (6.9). This crossover reflects the
value of deep permutation-tested trees: features discovered at deeper nodes
contribute meaningful signal at larger $k$, whereas RF's impurity-based
importance concentrates on the strongest signals and adds diminishing value in
the tail.

\subsubsection{Ablation study}
\label{sec:experiments-ablation}

We systematically vary each design parameter of the conditional-inference forest
to isolate its effect on ranking quality and runtime.

\paragraph{Sensitivity to $\alpha$.}
\Cref{fig:ablation-alpha} shows precision@10 across $\alpha_{\text{selector}}
\in \{10^{-3}, 10^{-2}, 0.05, 0.1, 0.2, 0.5\}$ on synthetic datasets. On
strong-signal data (Toeplitz, easy), precision is essentially flat across all
$\alpha$ values (0.86--0.92), confirming that the permutation test is
informative enough to identify strong features regardless of the threshold. On
weak-signal and high-dimensional data, a more permissive $\alpha$ marginally
improves precision (e.g., 0.10 at $\alpha{=}0.01$ vs.\ 0.20 at $\alpha{=}0.2$
on clf\_weak\_signal) at the cost of admitting more noise features.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{figures/ablation_alpha_depth.png}
  \caption{Precision@10 vs.\ $\alpha_{\text{selector}}$ across synthetic
  datasets. Strong-signal datasets are insensitive to $\alpha$; weak-signal
  datasets benefit marginally from more permissive thresholds.}
  \label{fig:ablation-alpha}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Forest size saturation.}
The number of estimators exhibits diminishing returns (\cref{fig:ablation-nest}).
On Toeplitz-correlated data, a single tree achieves 0.44 precision@10;
$n_{\text{est}}{=}25$ saturates at 0.90, with no improvement through
$n_{\text{est}}{=}100$. On weaker-signal and confounder datasets, the marginal
gain from 25 to 100 trees is ${\leq}0.04$ precision while runtime nearly
quadruples. We recommend $n_{\text{est}}{=}100$ as the default for robustness,
with 25 as a practical budget-constrained alternative.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{figures/ablation_n_estimators.png}
  \caption{Precision@10 vs.\ number of estimators. Performance saturates
  by $n_{\text{est}}{=}25$ on correlated data and shows diminishing returns
  on harder datasets.}
  \label{fig:ablation-nest}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Permutation budget.}
\Cref{fig:ablation-nresamples} shows that $B{=}99$ permutations with adaptive
stopping achieves virtually identical ranking quality to $B{=}999$.
On clf\_toeplitz: 0.90 at $B{=}49$ vs.\ 0.92 at $B{=}99$ and 0.90 at
$B{=}999$. On reg\_weak\_signal: 0.76 across all $B$ values. The adaptive
stopping rule extracts sufficient information from small permutation budgets,
making the ``minimum'' preset ($B{=}49$--$99$) a practical default.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{figures/ablation_nresamples.png}
  \caption{Precision@10 vs.\ permutation budget $B$. Results are essentially
  flat beyond $B{=}99$, confirming that adaptive stopping makes large
  permutation budgets unnecessary.}
  \label{fig:ablation-nresamples}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Bootstrap and feature subsampling.}
\Cref{fig:ablation-bootstrap} reveals that bootstrap sampling and
feature subsampling interact with dataset structure. Disabling bootstrap
yields perfect precision (1.00) on the easy dataset compared to 0.84 with
bootstrap, because bagging introduces ranking variability. However, on the
confounder dataset, half-feature subsampling ($p/2$) improves precision from
0.38 to 0.60 and reduces the confounder rate in the top-5 from 0.64 to 0.24,
because forcing each tree to evaluate different feature subsets breaks the
correlation that allows confounders to dominate.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/ablation_bootstrap.png}
  \caption{Precision@10 across bootstrap and feature subsampling
  strategies. No-bootstrap is better on easy/strong-signal data; $p/2$
  subsampling substantially helps on confounder-contaminated data.}
  \label{fig:ablation-bootstrap}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Noise robustness.}
When 1000 pure-noise features are injected (100:1 noise-to-signal ratio), CIF
achieves 0.70 precision@10 on strong-signal data, behind RF (0.80) and ET
(0.80) but still identifying 7 of 10 informative features. On moderate signal
CIF drops to 0.50 precision. When the number of informative features is
increased to 20, all three methods achieve perfect precision@10. CIF's runtime
remains near-constant at ${\sim}31$\,s regardless of difficulty, consistent with
the feature-muting mechanism rapidly pruning noise features from consideration.
