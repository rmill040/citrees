On the classification benchmark with 14 datasets, the 95\% bootstrap intervals

\subsection{Runtime ablations}
\label{sec:results-practical}

These comparisons change one setting at a time and compare runtime to the
corresponding default method. All timing entries below are ratios: values above
1 mean slower than the default, and values below 1 mean faster. CIT and CIF are
reported separately because they have different measurements. The CIT timing run
records fit/ranking time and synthetic top-10 recovery, but does not rerun
downstream classifiers or regressors. The CIF ablations include both scores on
real datasets and synthetic recovery summaries.

  \centering
  \footnotesize
  \setlength{\tabcolsep}{4pt}
  \begin{tabularx}{\linewidth}{@{}>{\raggedright\arraybackslash}X c c@{}}
    \toprule
    CIT change & Runtime ratio & Synthetic top-10 F1 change \\
    \midrule
    Disable adaptive stopping & 0.18--1.14$\times$ & $\le 0.026$ \\
    Exact threshold search & 0.98--2.51$\times$ & $\le 0.008$ \\
    Disable feature scanning & 0.69--1.46$\times$ & $\le 0.017$ \\
    \bottomrule

  against the default CIT setting and summarized across the real and synthetic

  display.}
  \label{tab:cit-runtime-controls}
\end{table}

\paragraph{CIT timing.}
\Cref{tab:cit-runtime-controls} shows that the CIT timing effects are mixed.
Disabling adaptive stopping is faster in the synthetic runs but not consistently
faster on real datasets, where the median ratios span both sides of 1. Exact
threshold search is sometimes slower and sometimes close to the default. Feature
scanning also does not give a clean runtime and recovery tradeoff. Because this run
did not refit downstream models, these rows should be read as timing and
synthetic recovery evidence, not as downstream performance evidence.

\paragraph{CIF timing and quality.}
The CIF ablation table combines timing ratios with score changes on real
datasets and synthetic recovery changes.

  \centering
  \footnotesize
  \setlength{\tabcolsep}{4pt}

  \caption{CIF runtime and quality ablations. Runtime ratios are medians of

  informative feature precision over the standard numbers of selected features.
  Values are rounded for display.}
  \label{tab:cif-runtime-controls}

\Cref{tab:cif-runtime-controls} shows a cleaner practical pattern for CIF.
Adaptive stopping is the main runtime control: disabling it makes fits
4.0--8.4$\times$ slower by the median ratio across datasets, while downstream
score and synthetic recovery changes remain small. Exact threshold search is
also slower than histogram-256, with ratios from 1.9--10.8$\times$. Feature
scanning is not a large standalone speed lever once adaptive stopping is
enabled.
Removing Bonferroni correction is faster for both CIT and CIF, but it changes
the statistical rule and grows broader trees, so it is a boundary diagnostic
rather than a practical recommendation.

\section{High dimensional and synthetic limits}
\label{sec:boundary}

We next check three boundary cases: where CIF scores peak in high-$p$ data,
whether synthetic recovery improves beyond the first ranked feature, and how
sampling candidate features inside the forest affects informative split
selection.

\paragraph{High-$p$ results on real datasets.}
Across the 15 classification datasets with more than 100 features, CIF first
reaches its best score between 100 and $p$ in 30 of 45 dataset and model
combinations. The first best score occurs at $k{=}p$ in only 5 of those 45
combinations. \Cref{fig:high-p-boundary}
shows this pattern separately by downstream learner. Scores most often peak at
intermediate values of $k$: 11 of 15 LR cells, 9 of 15 SVM cells, and 10 of 15
KNN cells first reach their best score at $100 < k < p$. The full feature set
$k=p$ is mainly an LR exception. For LR, moving from $k{=}100$ to $k{=}p$
improves CIF on average by $+0.022$, and $k=p$ matches the best observed score
in 6 of 15 cells. For SVM and KNN, the corresponding mean changes are
$-0.058$ and $-0.075$, and $k=p$ is best in only 1 of 15 cells each.

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/high_p_boundary_summary.png}
  \caption{Classification high-$p$ boundary for CIF by downstream learner.
  Each cell is one high-$p$ classification dataset and one downstream model.
  (A) Share of cells where the first evaluated $k$ attaining the best

  $k{=}p$. (B) Mean balanced accuracy change from $k{=}100$ to $k{=}p$.}

The regression results are smaller and less favorable. Across the 6
regression datasets with more than 100 features, 11 of 18 dataset and model
combinations peak at or below $k{=}100$, only 2 peak at $k=p$, and the mean
$k{=}p$ minus $k{=}100$ score change is $-0.308$. Using all features is
therefore usually not helpful, and the $k=p$ exception is mainly a
classification LR phenomenon rather than a general CIF pattern.

\paragraph{Synthetic feature recovery.}
Across the classification synthetic datasets, CIF ranks 8th of 17 by the share
of informative features selected. Its top-1 hit rate is 0.64, which places it
12th of 17 by top-1 hit rate. CIF is therefore stronger at enriching the
selected top-$k$ feature set with informative features than at placing an
informative feature first.
\Cref{tab:synthetic-head}
summarizes this contrast, and \Cref{fig:synthetic-topk-curves}
shows how the selected set changes as $k$ grows. The share of
informative features in the selected set falls as $k$ grows. On the redundant classification synthetic datasets, 83\% of the selected
features are still signal variables or redundant proxies rather than noise
features.

\paragraph{Candidate feature sampling limit.}
This simulation asks whether the forest step that samples candidate features,
rather than Stage~A itself, explains missed informative splits.
\Cref{fig:mechanism-forest-counts} shows one sparse classification design where
default CIF samples candidate features and the CIF variant with all features
available makes every feature available at each split.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/paper_mechanism_grid_forest_classification_feature_counts_p1000_i2_1000trees.png}
  \caption{Feature use counts in the forest on the fixed sparse
  classification design with $n{=}250$, $p{=}1{,}000$, two informative features,
  and 1,000 trees. Each count is the number of internal splits using a feature.
  Default CIF samples candidate features; the CIF variant with all features
  available makes all features available at each split. Vertical scales differ
  by panel.}
  \label{fig:mechanism-forest-counts}
\end{figure}

In single trees, CIT selects informative splits at rates of 1.000 on
classification and 0.935 on regression. This indicates that Stage~A can identify
informative features when all features are available. The drop appears after
the forest samples candidate features. On the classification simulations with
1,000 trees, default CIF selects informative splits 33.2\% of the time, while
CIF with all features available reaches 91.7\%, RF reaches 8.4\%, and ET reaches 6.5\%. Default CIF
also uses an average of 265 distinct uninformative features, versus 93 for
the CIF variant with all features available. In regression,
the CIF variant with all features available selects informative splits 42.3\% of the time, versus 27.8\% for
default CIF. It also uses more distinct uninformative features: 528.8 versus
367.7. These results suggest that conditional inference feature selection is
most reliable when informative features are included among the candidate split
features often enough. In sparse high-$p$ settings, sampling candidate features
inside the forest can leave them out too often at a node.
