\subsection{Runtime ablations}
\label{sec:results-practical}

These comparisons change one setting at a time and compare runtime to the
corresponding default method. All runtime numbers below are ratios: values above
1 mean slower than the default, and values below 1 mean faster. CIT and CIF are
reported separately because they have different measurements. The CIT timing run
records fit/ranking time and synthetic top-10 recovery, but does not rerun
downstream classifiers or regressors. The CIF ablations include downstream
metric changes and synthetic recovery results.

\paragraph{CIT timing.}
\Cref{tab:cit-runtime-controls} shows that the CIT timing effects are mixed.
Disabling adaptive stopping is faster in the synthetic runs but not consistently
faster on real datasets, where the median ratios span both sides of 1. Exact
threshold search is sometimes slower and sometimes close to the default. Feature
scanning also does not give a consistent tradeoff between runtime and synthetic
recovery. Because this run did not refit downstream models, these rows report
timing and synthetic-recovery changes, not downstream performance.

The CIF ablation table combines timing ratios with downstream metric changes

\Cref{tab:cif-runtime-controls} shows a more consistent CIF runtime pattern.
Adaptive stopping is the main runtime control: disabling it makes fits
4.0--8.4$\times$ slower by the median ratio across datasets, while downstream
metric and synthetic recovery changes remain small. Exact threshold search is
also slower than histogram-256, with ratios from 1.9--10.8$\times$. Feature
scanning has little separate runtime effect once adaptive stopping is enabled.
Removing Bonferroni correction is faster for both CIT and CIF, but it changes
the statistical rule and produces trees that use more features, so it is a
diagnostic sensitivity comparison rather than a practical recommendation.

\section{\texorpdfstring{High-$p$}{High-p} and synthetic behavior}
\label{sec:boundary}

The remaining analyses study where CIF's scores peak as the selected feature
set grows, how synthetic recovery changes across values of $k$, and how
candidate feature sampling inside the forest affects informative split
selection.

\paragraph{High-$p$ results on real datasets.}
Across the 15 classification datasets with more than 100 features, CIF first
reaches its best score between 100 and $p$ in 30 of 45 dataset-learner pairs.
The first best score occurs at $k{=}p$ in only 5 of those 45 pairs.
\Cref{fig:high-p-boundary}
shows this pattern separately by downstream learner. Scores most often peak at
intermediate values of $k$: 11 of 15 LR datasets, 9 of 15 SVM datasets, and 10
of 15 KNN datasets first reach their best score at $100 < k < p$. The full feature set
$k=p$ is mainly an LR exception. For LR, moving from $k{=}100$ to $k{=}p$
improves CIF on average by $+0.022$, and $k=p$ matches the best observed score
in 6 of 15 datasets. For SVM and KNN, the corresponding mean changes are
$-0.058$ and $-0.075$, and $k=p$ is best in only 1 of 15 datasets for each
learner.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/high_p_boundary_summary.png}
  \caption{Classification high-$p$ boundary for CIF by downstream learner.
  Panel A gives, for each learner, the share of 15 datasets where the first
  evaluated $k$ attaining the best observed CIF score falls below 100, at 100,
  between 100 and $p$, or at $k{=}p$. Panel B gives the mean balanced accuracy
  change from $k{=}100$ to $k{=}p$.}
  \label{fig:high-p-boundary}
\end{figure}

In the 6 high-$p$ regression datasets, the pattern is more mixed. Across 18
dataset-learner pairs, 11 peak at or below $k{=}100$, only 2 peak at $k=p$, and
the mean $k{=}p$ minus $k{=}100$ score change is $-0.308$. In this subset, using
the full feature set is not generally helpful, and the $k=p$ exception appears mainly in
classification with LR.

\paragraph{Synthetic feature recovery.}
Across the classification synthetic datasets, CIF ranks 8th of 17 by the share
of informative features selected. Its top-1 hit rate is 0.64, which places it
12th of 17 by top-1 hit rate. CIF is therefore stronger at enriching the
selected top-$k$ feature set with informative features than at placing an
informative feature first.
For regression, CIF ranks 10th of 18 by informative share and 15th of 18 by
top-1 hit rate.
\Cref{tab:synthetic-head}
summarizes this contrast, and \Cref{fig:synthetic-topk-curves}
shows how the selected set changes as $k$ grows. The share of
informative features in the selected set falls as $k$ grows. On the
redundant-feature synthetic designs, most selected features are still signal
variables or redundant proxies rather than noise features: 0.83 for
classification and 0.84 for regression.

\paragraph{Candidate feature sampling mechanism.}
The separate mechanism grid asks whether the forest step that samples candidate
features contributes to missed informative splits in sparse forests.
\Cref{fig:mechanism-forest-counts} shows one sparse classification design where
default CIF samples candidate features, whereas CIF-all sends every nonconstant
candidate to Stage~A at each split.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/paper_mechanism_grid_forest_classification_feature_counts_p1000_i2_1000trees.png}
  \caption{Feature use counts in the forest on the single sparse
  classification design with $n{=}250$, $p{=}1{,}000$, two informative features,
  and 1,000 trees. Each count is the number of internal splits using a feature.
  Default CIF samples candidate features; CIF-all sends every nonconstant
  candidate to Stage~A at each split. Vertical scales differ by panel.}
  \label{fig:mechanism-forest-counts}
\end{figure}

Across the single-tree mechanism grid, CIT selects informative splits at rates
of 1.000 on classification and 0.935 on regression, showing that, in this fixed
design, exposing all candidate features yields many informative splits. In forests,
the informative split share falls when only a subset of candidate features is
sampled at each node. Across the classification forest mechanism grid at 1,000
trees, default CIF selects informative splits 33.2\% of the time, compared with
91.7\% for CIF-all, 8.4\% for RF, and 6.5\% for ET. Default CIF also uses more
distinct noise features than CIF-all, 265 versus 93 on average. The displayed
slice with $p{=}1{,}000$ and two informative features is an extreme case of the
candidate sampling mechanism: default CIF selects informative splits at rate 0.090, while
CIF-all selects them at rate 1.000. In regression, CIF-all selects informative
splits 42.3\% of the time, versus 27.8\% for default CIF, but it uses more
distinct noise features: 528.8 versus 367.7. CIF-all changes candidate feature
availability by setting \texttt{max\_features=None}; feature muting and the
other tree controls remain in force. In these sparse high-$p$ simulations, CIF
has its highest informative split share when informative features are included
among the candidate split features at many nodes. When informative
features are rarely sampled into candidate sets at nodes, Stage~A cannot
select them.
