\paragraph{Regression benchmark.}
On the regression surface, CIF is 2nd of 16 on the changing-support

\subsection{Practical controls}
\label{sec:results-practical}

The practical ablations identify two main runtime levers: adaptive stopping
and histogram thresholding. Once adaptive stopping is enabled, feature
scanning contributes only a modest additional gain. We vary adaptive stopping, threshold budgets, feature scanning,
Bonferroni correction, threshold scanning, muting, and bootstrap in matched
CIF-only comparisons. These ablations isolate changes within CIF rather than
rerun the full benchmark.

    5.1--11.7$\times$ slower &

    \bottomrule

  \label{tab:practical-controls}

Numerically, disabling adaptive stopping is 5.1--11.7$\times$ slower with
near-zero downstream score change. Replacing histogram-256 with exact
threshold search is 1.9--10.6$\times$ slower with similarly small downstream
score changes. With adaptive stopping held fixed, removing feature scanning is
1.02--1.22$\times$ slower across the matched datasets, while downstream score
changes stay between about $-0.0055$ and $+0.0006$. Removing Bonferroni correction speeds up CIF
but makes trees much deeper. The other ablations are less
consistent. Removing threshold scanning changes runtime by
0.69--1.07$\times$. Its downstream score changes are small. Muting has
almost no effect on runtime: 0.97--1.00$\times$. Its downstream
score changes are negligible. Removing bootstrap changes depth and feature use
without a consistent speed change. Runtime ranges from 0.89--1.36$\times$, and downstream
score changes are mixed. A smaller histogram budget can reduce runtime further.
The histogram-256 default remains much faster than exact threshold search.

\section{Boundary diagnostics}
\label{sec:boundary}

The boundary diagnostics identify three patterns. On the high-$p$ classification datasets,
CIF often performs best at intermediate feature budgets. Its subset
construction is stronger than its exact head-of-list recovery, and it weakens when
forest subsampling keeps informative features out of the candidate set too often.

\paragraph{High-$p$ real-data saturation.}
Across the 15 classification datasets with more than 100 features, CIF first
reaches its best score between 100 and $p$ in 30 of 45
dataset and model combinations. Only 5 of 45 first do so at $k{=}p$. On
the regression datasets, 11 of 18 dataset and model combinations peak at or below $k{=}100$.
Another 5 peak between 100 and $p$. Only 2 peak at the endpoint. \Cref{tab:high-p-cif} shows that
CIF often improves when the feature budget moves beyond the smallest settings.
Using all features usually does not help. On average, moving from $k{=}100$ to $k{=}p$ lowers CIF's score.

Across the classification synthetic datasets, CIF ranks 8th of 15 by the share
of informative features selected. Its top-1 hit rate is 0.64, which places it
tied 11th--12th of 15 by top-1 hit rate. CIF is better at building a useful top-$k$ set than
at finding the exact top-ranked feature. \Cref{tab:synthetic-head}
summarizes this contrast, and \Cref{fig:synthetic-topk-curves}
shows how the selected set changes as $k$ grows. The share of
informative features in the selected set falls as $k$ grows. On the redundant classification synthetic datasets, 83\% of the selected
features are still signal or redundant rather than pure noise.

\paragraph{Candidate-set exposure.}
The fixed-design study helps explain this limit. In single trees, CIT selects
informative splits at rates of 1.000 on classification and 0.935 on regression. Stage~A works well when
all features are available. Most of the loss in informative-split selection appears after forest subsampling. On
the 1000-tree classification grid, default CIF selects informative splits 33.2\%
of the time, while \texttt{cif\_all} reaches 91.7\%, RF reaches 8.4\%, and ET reaches 6.5\%.
Default CIF also uses an average of 265 distinct false features, versus 93 for
\texttt{cif\_all}. In regression,
\texttt{cif\_all} selects informative splits 42.3\% of the time, versus 27.8\%
for default CIF. It also uses more distinct false features:
528.8 versus 367.7. Conditional-inference feature selection works when
informative features enter the candidate set often enough. In sparse high-$p$ settings,
forest subsampling can leave them out of the candidate set too often at a node.
