configurations.

second by average LODO rank (mean LODO rank 5.50), behind ExtraTrees, and CIF

\subsection{Runtime ablations}
\label{sec:results-practical}

These comparisons change one setting at a time and compare runtime to the
corresponding default method. All runtime numbers below are ratios: values above
1 mean slower than the default, and values below 1 mean faster. CIT and CIF are
reported separately because the CIT timing run and CIF ablations record
different outputs. The CIT timing run
records fit/ranking time and top-10 feature recovery on synthetic datasets, but
does not rerun downstream classifiers or regressors. The CIF ablations include
downstream metric changes and feature recovery results on synthetic datasets.
The accounting in \cref{app:complexity-details} identifies the affected work
terms; the ablations measure the corresponding observed timing and metric
changes.

\paragraph{CIT timing.}
\Cref{tab:cit-runtime-hyperparams} shows that the CIT timing effects are mixed.
Disabling adaptive stopping is faster in the synthetic runs but not consistently
faster on real datasets, where the median ratios span both sides of 1. Exact
threshold search is sometimes slower and sometimes close to the default. Feature
scanning also does not give a consistent tradeoff between runtime and top-10
feature recovery on synthetic datasets. Because this run did not refit downstream
models, these rows report timing and feature recovery changes on synthetic
datasets, not downstream performance.

\Cref{tab:cif-runtime-hyperparams} shows a more consistent CIF timing pattern.
Disabling adaptive stopping makes fits 4.0--8.4$\times$ slower by the median

sensitivity comparison rather than a recommended setting.

\section{\texorpdfstring{High-$p$}{High-p} and synthetic behavior}
\label{sec:boundary}

The remaining analyses study where CIF's scores peak as the selected feature
set grows, how feature recovery on synthetic datasets changes across values of
$k$, and how candidate feature sampling inside the forest affects informative
split selection.

\paragraph{High-$p$ results on real datasets.}
Across the 15 classification datasets with more than 100 features, CIF first
reaches its best score between 100 and $p$ in 30 of the 45 combinations of
dataset and downstream learner. The first best score occurs at $k{=}p$ in only
5 of those 45 combinations.
\Cref{fig:high-p-boundary} shows this classification pattern separately by
downstream learner; the smaller regression high-$p$ result is summarized after
the figure. Classification scores most often peak at intermediate values of
$k$: 11 of 15 LR datasets, 9 of 15 SVM datasets, and 10 of 15 KNN datasets first
reach their best score at $100 < k < p$. The full feature set $k=p$ is mainly an
LR exception. For LR, moving from $k{=}100$ to $k{=}p$ improves CIF on average
by $+0.022$, and $k=p$ matches the best observed score in 6 of 15 datasets. For
SVM and KNN, the corresponding mean changes are $-0.058$ and $-0.075$, and
$k=p$ is best in only 1 of 15 datasets for each learner.

In the 6 high-$p$ regression datasets, the pattern is more mixed. Across the 18
combinations of dataset and downstream learner, 11 peak at or below $k{=}100$,
only 2 peak at $k=p$, and the mean $k{=}p$ minus $k{=}100$ score change is
$-0.308$. In this smaller regression subset, using the full feature set is not
generally helpful. The main $k=p$ exception is the classification LR result in
\Cref{fig:high-p-boundary}.

\paragraph{Synthetic feature recovery.}
Across the classification synthetic datasets, CIF's mean informative feature
share over the evaluated top-$k$ sets is 0.34, ranked 8th of 17. Its top-1 hit
rate is 0.64, ranked 12th of 17. For regression, CIF's mean informative feature
share is 0.39, ranked 10th of 18, and its top-1 hit rate is 0.81, ranked 15th
of 18. In both tasks, CIF ranks higher by mean informative share than by top-1
hit rate, so the synthetic result is more favorable for top-$k$ enrichment than
for the first ranked feature.
\Cref{tab:synthetic-head}
summarizes this contrast, and \Cref{fig:synthetic-topk-curves}
shows how the selected set changes as $k$ grows. The share of
informative features in the selected set falls as $k$ grows. On the
redundant-feature synthetic designs, most selected features are still signal
variables or redundant proxies rather than noise features: 0.83 for
classification and 0.84 for regression.

\paragraph{Candidate feature sampling.}
A diagnostic simulation grid examines whether candidate feature sampling in
forests contributes to missed informative splits in sparse classification and
regression settings. \Cref{fig:candidate-forest-counts} shows one sparse
classification design where default CIF samples candidate features, whereas
CIF-all sets
\texttt{max\_features=None} so every nonconstant candidate can enter Stage~A at
each split. Appendix~\ref{app:candidate-availability} gives the classification
and regression results over $p = 100, 500$, and $1{,}000$.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/paper_mechanism_grid_forest_classification_feature_counts_p1000_i2_1000trees.png}
  \caption{Tree-level feature use in the forest on the single sparse
  classification design with $n{=}250$, $p{=}1{,}000$, two informative features,
  and 1,000 trees. Each count is the number of trees in which the feature is
  used in at least one internal split.
  Default CIF samples candidate features; CIF-all sets
  \texttt{max\_features=None}. Vertical scales differ by panel.}
  \label{fig:candidate-forest-counts}
\end{figure}

Across the single-tree simulation grid, CIT selects informative splits at rates
of 1.000 on classification and 0.935 on regression, showing that, in this fixed
design, the rate of informative splits is high when all nonconstant features are
considered. In forests,
informative splits become less frequent when only a subset of candidate features
is sampled at each node. Across the classification forest simulation grid at 1,000
trees, default CIF selects informative splits 33.2\% of the time, compared with
91.7\% for CIF-all, 8.4\% for RF, and 6.5\% for ET. Default CIF also uses more
distinct noise features than CIF-all, 265 versus 93 on average. The displayed
slice with $p{=}1{,}000$ and two informative features shows the effect clearly:
default CIF selects informative splits at rate 0.090, while CIF-all selects them
at rate 1.000. In regression, CIF-all
selects informative splits 42.3\% of the time, versus 27.8\% for default CIF,
but it uses more
distinct noise features: 528.8 versus 367.7. The CIF-all results are used here
to isolate the effect of candidate feature sampling, not as a separate benchmark
setting.
Feature muting and all other tree settings are held fixed. In these sparse
high-$p$ simulations, informative splits are most frequent when all nonconstant
features can enter Stage~A. When candidate sampling rarely includes
an informative feature at a node, Stage~A cannot select that feature.
