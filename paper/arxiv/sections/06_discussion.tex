\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

On the real-data benchmarks, the clearest empirical signal is on classification,
where CIF consistently improves on the historical conditional-inference
baselines. The fixed-node theorem isolates
the fixed-node Stage~A result that remains valid at that node, and the
practical
ablations show where computation can be reduced without materially changing
feature-ranking performance. Together, those results support CIF as a screening-and-ranking method on
the classification benchmark.

The additional analyses also clarify where the method weakens. The basic Stage~A
feature-selection rule is not the problem: when all features are visible, CIT
is highly selective. The problem appears after forest
subsampling in sparse high-dimensional settings, where exposure to informative
features becomes too limited. This mechanism explains the
boundary patterns reported in \cref{sec:boundary}.

\paragraph{Practical guidance.}
Use CIF when the task is feature ranking or feature selection, when nonlinear
or interaction structure matters, and when a statistically grounded feature-selection
step is part of why the method is used. The default benchmark configuration
keeps adaptive stopping, histogram thresholding for threshold search, and
Bonferroni when the goal is a stricter, more selective learner rather than a
faster but much less selective one. We use Bonferroni because the focus is
strict family-wise control for Stage~A, whereas
dependence-aware resampling adjustments such as Westfall--Young
\citep{WestfallYoung1993ResamplingBasedMultipleTesting} or discovery-oriented
FDR rules such as Benjamini--Hochberg
\citep{BenjaminiHochberg1995FDR} would answer a different question and are
outside this benchmark. Feature scanning should also stay on by default:
once adaptive stopping is enabled, it adds a modest runtime gain with only
small and mixed score changes. Threshold scanning has mixed effects and is not
a practical priority. Muting has little effect on runtime, and bootstrap
changes forest behavior without yielding a consistent speedup. If pure
predictive accuracy is the only objective, strong generic ensembles remain
competitive alternatives on the classification benchmark.
When the objective is variable screening or ranking rather than final prediction,
CIF should also be read against forest-based variable-selection procedures
\citep{GenuerPoggiTuleauMalot2010VariableSelectionUsingRandomForests}, not only
against end-to-end predictive ensembles.

\paragraph{Boundaries.}
The 14-dataset classification benchmark covers 14 of the 23 datasets, so the most
controlled benchmark is narrower than the full benchmark. Regression
evidence is useful but still limited at 8 datasets. The simulation study
sheds light on exposure limits in high-$p$ forests, but it does not manipulate
that process directly. Improving candidate-set exposure inside forests is therefore the clearest next
step.
