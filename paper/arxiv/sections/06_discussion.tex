\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The benchmark results favor CIF on average, with the clearest evidence in
classification. Across the 22 classification datasets with ctree and cforest
results, CIF has higher average downstream scores than both methods. In
regression, CIF has higher average downstream scores than ctree, cforest, and
CIT across 8 datasets; the complete-case regression rank analysis uses 6
datasets and has wider intervals. The theorem covers the
fixed-node Stage~A test with fixed features and fixed budget, independent of the
node responses under the complete null; the benchmark results cover fitted tree
and forest rankings. The timing ablations show the largest changes for adaptive
stopping and exact threshold search, with small score changes.

The CIF ablation compares split-count ranking, feature muting, bootstrap
sampling, and one-tree variants under the same top-$k$ evaluation. Replacing
split-importance ranking with split counts and disabling feature muting change
average scores little. Removing bootstrap sampling is close to neutral in
classification and worse in regression. Reducing the forest to one tree gives the
largest score change in both tasks.

The high-$p$ and synthetic results point to one reason CIF can struggle in sparse
forests. In the simulation with candidate sampling, single-tree CIT often selects
informative features for splits because Stage~A considers all nonconstant
features. In sparse high-$p$ forests, candidate sampling can leave informative
features out of many split decisions. The effect is clearest in the
classification forest simulations. Regression shows the same availability issue,
but the change in uninformative feature use is smaller and less consistent. This
matches the results in \cref{sec:boundary}.

The default benchmark keeps adaptive stopping, histogram thresholding, and
Bonferroni correction. Bonferroni correction makes accepted splits require
smaller adjusted $p$-values and tends to produce trees that use fewer features;
removing it is included as a sensitivity check. Dependence-aware resampling,
such as Westfall--Young \citep{WestfallYoung1993ResamplingBasedMultipleTesting},
or false discovery rate control, such as Benjamini--Hochberg
\citep{BenjaminiHochberg1995FDR}, would target different error criteria and are
worth testing separately. Feature and threshold scanning play a smaller role in
these experiments: once adaptive stopping is enabled, their timing effects are
mixed and their score changes are small.

\paragraph{Scope and next steps.}
The complete-case analyses are smaller than the full benchmark: 14
classification datasets for all 17 methods and 6 regression datasets for the
analogous rank summary. The ctree and cforest classification comparisons use
22 datasets, and the regression main table uses 8. The simulations also show
that high-$p$ forests can miss informative features when candidate sampling
leaves those features unavailable at many splits. A direct next step is to test
forest variants that consider more candidate features at each split.

\paragraph{Practical guidance.}
CIF is most relevant when the goal is an ordered feature list or selected
feature subset, especially when nonlinear effects or interactions may matter.
For pure prediction accuracy, boosted trees and random forest baselines should
remain in the comparison set. For variable screening or ranking, CIF should also
be compared with forest-based variable-selection methods
\citep{GenuerPoggiTuleauMalot2010VariableSelectionUsingRandomForests} alongside
ensembles built mainly for prediction.
