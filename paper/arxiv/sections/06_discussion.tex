\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The paper supports a clear claim. Conditional inference becomes much more
useful once its expensive defaults are modernized, and the resulting CIF is an
effective feature-ranking method. The theorem gives a
clean fixed-node feature-selection guarantee, the practical ablations show how
to preserve useful ranking behavior without paying unnecessary cost, and the benchmark shows
that the resulting learner is the strongest conditional-inference method we
tested while remaining competitive with standard tree ensembles.

The diagnostic results explain these limits without changing that
claim. The weak point is not the basic Stage~A feature-selection rule; when all
features are visible, CIT is highly selective. The weak point is forest
subsampling in sparse high-dimensional settings, where informative features do
not enter the candidate set often enough. That is why CIF performs best at
intermediate feature budgets and why the synthetic datasets favor subset
construction over identifying the exact top-ranked feature.

\paragraph{Practical guidance.}
Use CIF when the task is feature ranking or feature selection, when nonlinear
or interaction structure matters, and when a statistically grounded feature-selection
step is part of why the method is used. The benchmarked defaults are: keep adaptive stopping as the main runtime control,
keep histogram thresholding as the threshold-search approximation, and keep Bonferroni
when the goal is a stricter, more selective learner rather than a faster but
much less selective one. Feature scanning should also stay on by default: with adaptive
stopping already enabled, it adds a modest extra runtime gain with only small
and mixed quality changes. Threshold scanning has mixed effects, so it should be viewed as an internal
ordering heuristic, not a practical priority. Muting has little effect on
runtime, and bootstrap changes the forest behavior without yielding a
consistent speedup. If pure predictive accuracy is the only objective, boosting
ensembles such as LightGBM and XGBoost remain the stronger default choice.

\paragraph{Boundaries.}
The fixed classification panel covers 14 of the 23 datasets, so the most
controlled benchmark is narrower than the full benchmark. Regression
evidence is useful but still limited at 8 datasets. The mechanism study supports
candidate-set exposure as the leading explanation for CIF's high-$p$ boundary,
but it does not directly manipulate feature exposure.
The next step is algorithmic: improve feature exposure inside
forests through deterministic cycling, stratified candidate sampling, or other
ways of making informative features visible more often without discarding the
conditional-inference feature-selection rule.
