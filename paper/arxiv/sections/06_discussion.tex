\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

On the real-data benchmarks, CIF consistently outperforms the historical
conditional-inference baselines, with the clearest evidence on classification.
The theorem gives a
clean fixed-node feature-selection guarantee. The practical ablations show
where computation can be reduced while keeping the ranking behavior largely
intact. The benchmark
results show that CIF is the strongest conditional-inference method and remains
competitive with strong generic ensembles.

The diagnostics also show where the method weakens. The basic Stage~A feature-selection rule is not the problem; when all
features are visible, CIT is highly selective. The problem appears after forest
subsampling in sparse high-dimensional settings, where informative features do
not enter the candidate set often enough. That is why CIF often improves as
feature budgets move beyond the smallest settings and why it is better at
building useful top-$k$ sets than at identifying the exact top-ranked feature.

\paragraph{Practical guidance.}
Use CIF when the task is feature ranking or feature selection, when nonlinear
or interaction structure matters, and when a statistically grounded feature-selection
step is part of why the method is used. The default benchmark configuration
keeps adaptive stopping, histogram thresholding for threshold search, and
Bonferroni when the goal is a stricter, more selective learner rather than a
faster but much less selective one. Feature scanning should also stay on by default: with adaptive
stopping already enabled, it adds a modest extra runtime gain with only small
and mixed score changes. Threshold scanning has mixed effects, so it is not a
practical priority. Muting has little effect on
runtime, and bootstrap changes the forest behavior without yielding a
consistent speedup. If pure predictive accuracy is the only objective, strong
generic ensembles are often competitive alternatives on the classification benchmark.

\paragraph{Boundaries.}
The fixed classification panel covers 14 of the 23 datasets, so the most
controlled benchmark is narrower than the full benchmark. Regression
evidence is useful but still limited at 8 datasets. The mechanism study suggests
that informative features enter the candidate set too rarely in high-$p$
forests, but it does not directly manipulate that process.
Improving candidate-set exposure inside forests is therefore the clearest next
step.
