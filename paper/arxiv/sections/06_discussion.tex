\section{Discussion and limitations}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{What is actually guaranteed.}
The scoped guarantees in this paper concern fixed-node Stage~A screening in
fixed-$B$ mode under the nodewise \emph{complete} permutation null. Under A0.1--A0.5,
the +1 Monte Carlo p-value is super-uniform and Bonferroni controls the
complete-null probability of any Stage~A rejection at the node. That is the
right interpretation of the theorem chain: conservative complete-null
calibration for a pre-specified screening problem, not a blanket inferential
guarantee for the entire recursively grown tree. See \cref{sec:pvalue-scope} for
the concise scope table.

\paragraph{What is outside that scope.}
Stage~B thresholds are tested after a feature has already been chosen with the
same labels, and internal nodes are themselves created by earlier
label-dependent splits. Without additional selective-inference machinery or
sample splitting, those reported ``p-values'' should be treated as algorithmic
statistics rather than calibrated inferential quantities
\citep{Berk2013ValidPostSelectionInference,Lee2016ExactPostSelectionInferenceLasso,LeebPotscher2015ConfidenceIntervalsAfterModelSelection}.
The same caution applies to adaptive early-stopping outputs: they may be useful
engineering diagnostics, but in this manuscript they are not given
optional-stopping validity claims.

\paragraph{Exchangeability is the key modeling choice.}
Permutation calibration is only as good as the permutation scheme. If the data
are clustered, longitudinal, blocked, or otherwise dependent, naive label
permutations need not preserve the null distribution. Likewise, with-replacement
bootstrap resampling can break the simple root-exchangeability picture used by
the fixed-node theory. In such settings, calibrated interpretation requires a
restricted randomization scheme matched to the data structure; the default
library behavior should then be viewed as algorithmic unless that design issue
is handled explicitly.

\paragraph{Multiplicity and alternative inferential targets.}
Bonferroni is used here because it is simple, conservative, and assumption-light
for the nodewise tested family. A Westfall--Young max-$T$ correction across
features \citep{WestfallYoung1993ResamplingBasedMultipleTesting} is one possible
next step and could reduce conservativeness when dependence across features is
exploited. More broadly, other feature-selection error criteria such as FDR
\citep{BenjaminiHochberg1995FDR}, knockoffs \citep{BarberCandes2015Knockoffs},
and stability selection \citep{MeinshausenBuhlmann2010StabilitySelection}
address different inferential goals than the fixed-node screening target studied
here.

\paragraph{Practical guidance.}
If the goal is to stay inside the paper's theorem-backed regime, use fixed-$B$
Stage~A testing with a label-independent tested family, and ensure the Monte
Carlo resolution is compatible with the thresholding rule, i.e.,
$\alpha_{\mathrm{sel}}/m_t > 1/(B+1)$. The implementation's conservative tie
rule should be retained, and label-adaptive modifications of the tested family
at the node of interest should be avoided. If the goal is predictive
performance or ranking quality at scale, then early stopping, scan ordering,
feature muting, threshold subsampling, and forest resampling are reasonable
engineering choices, but their outputs should be justified empirically rather
than presented as covered by the fixed-node theory.
