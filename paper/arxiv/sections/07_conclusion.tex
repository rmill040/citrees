\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper studies how CIT and CIF runtime controls affect feature rankings. At a
fixed node with a fixed permutation budget and under the nodewise complete null,
the Stage~A result gives Bonferroni control of the nodewise rejection
probability.
That p-value interpretation applies to the local test; the full adaptive tree
and forest are evaluated empirically. In the CIF ablations, the clearest
runtime controls are adaptive stopping and histogram thresholding. The CIT
timing results are mixed and are therefore reported separately from the CIF
runtime and downstream score results.

On the classification benchmark, CIF ranks fourth of 17 methods with the runtime
controls studied here. It improves on ctree and cforest on the 22 classification
datasets where those references were run, and on CIT across 23 datasets.
The high-$p$ and synthetic results show two patterns: CIF is stronger at
enriching the selected top-$k$ feature set with informative features than at
placing an informative feature first, and the classification simulations point
to candidate sampling inside the forest as an important sparse high-$p$
constraint in the diagnostic simulations. The regression evidence is based on
fewer datasets and shows less consistent ranking gains. Further progress likely
depends on making informative features available at split decisions more often
while preserving the Stage~A/Stage~B separation that makes the method
distinctive.
