\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper studies how runtime hyperparameters in CIT and CIF affect
feature rankings, downstream prediction, and synthetic feature recovery. For
exhaustive fixed-$B$ Stage~A testing at a fixed node, with
a label-independent tested feature set, the Stage~A result gives Bonferroni
control of nodewise rejection under the complete nodewise permutation null.
That $p$-value interpretation applies to the local test; the full adaptive tree
and forest are evaluated empirically. In the CIF ablations, adaptive stopping
and histogram thresholding produce the largest timing changes. The CIT timing
results are mixed and are reported separately from the CIF runtime and
downstream prediction results.

On the real-data benchmarks, CIF ranks fourth of 17 classification methods and
third of 18 regression methods with the runtime hyperparameters studied here. In
direct comparisons, its average downstream scores are higher than ctree and
cforest across 22 classification datasets and higher than CIT across 23
classification datasets; on regression, CIF is higher than ctree and cforest on
7 of 8 datasets and higher than CIT on 6 of 8. The high-$p$ and synthetic
results show two patterns: the synthetic results are stronger for selected
feature sets than for the first ranked feature, and in sparse high-$p$
simulations, candidate sampling inside forests can leave informative features
unavailable at many split decisions. The regression evidence is based on fewer
datasets and has wider uncertainty intervals in the 6-dataset regression
analysis, but it is included throughout the empirical results. A natural next
step is to test forest variants that consider more candidate features at each
split while preserving the Stage~A/Stage~B separation that makes the method
distinctive.
