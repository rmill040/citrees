\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper studies how CIT and CIF runtime controls affect feature rankings. At
the level of a single node before later tree decisions are considered, the
Stage~A result gives Bonferroni control of nodewise rejection when no candidate
feature at that node is associated with the response and the permutation budget
is fixed.
That p-value interpretation applies to the local test; the full adaptive tree
and forest are evaluated empirically. In the CIF ablations, the largest runtime
changes come from adaptive stopping and histogram thresholding. The CIT timing
results are mixed and are therefore reported separately from the CIF runtime and
downstream score results.

On the classification benchmark, CIF ranks fourth of 17 methods with the runtime
controls studied here. It improves on ctree and cforest on the 22 classification
datasets where those references were run, and on CIT across 23 datasets.
The high-$p$ and synthetic results show two patterns: the gains are clearer for
enriching the selected top-$k$ feature set with informative features than for
placing an informative feature first, and the classification simulations point
to candidate sampling inside the forest as an important sparse high-$p$
constraint in the diagnostic simulations. The regression evidence is based on
fewer datasets and shows less consistent ranking gains. Further progress likely
depends on making informative features available at split decisions more often
while preserving the Stage~A/Stage~B separation that makes the method
distinctive.
