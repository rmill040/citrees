\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper isolates a principled core of conditional inference and shows how
to make it computationally practical. At a pre-specified node, the exhaustive fixed-$B$ Stage~A
$+1$ Monte Carlo p-value is super-uniform, and Bonferroni controls the
probability of any Stage~A rejection in the tested family. In CIF,
adaptive stopping provides the largest speedup with near-zero
score change; histogram thresholding adds a second speedup by bounding
threshold search; feature scanning adds a smaller gain once early stopping is
active; and threshold scanning, muting, and bootstrap have smaller or less
consistent effects on speed.

Empirically, CIF is the strongest conditional-inference method in the benchmark.
It clearly improves on the historical conditional-inference baselines and
remains competitive with several strong generic ensembles on the classification
benchmark. The boundary diagnostics clarify where that pattern weakens: in
classification, CIF often benefits from moving beyond the smallest feature
budgets, is better at building useful top-$k$ sets than at identifying the
exact top-ranked feature, and weakens in sparse high-$p$ settings when forest
subsampling makes informative features too rare in the candidate set. Further
progress likely depends on improving candidate-set exposure inside forests
while preserving the nodewise feature-selection rule that makes the method
distinctive.
