\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper isolates a principled core of conditional inference and shows how
to make it computationally practical. At a pre-specified node, the exhaustive fixed-$B$ Stage~A
$+1$ Monte Carlo p-value is super-uniform, and Bonferroni controls the
probability of any Stage~A rejection in the tested family. In CIF,
adaptive stopping provides the largest speedup with near-zero
score loss, histogram thresholding provides a second speedup by bounding threshold search,
feature scanning provides a smaller additional speedup inside early stopping, and
threshold scanning, muting, and bootstrap have smaller effects on speed.

Empirically, CIF is the strongest conditional-inference method in the benchmark.
It clearly improves on the historical conditional-inference baselines and
remains competitive with strong generic ensembles in the real-data benchmark. The
boundary diagnostics show where that pattern weakens: in classification, CIF
often improves beyond the smallest budgets, is better at building useful
top-$k$ sets than at identifying the exact top-ranked feature, and weakens
in sparse high-$p$ settings when forest subsampling makes informative features
too hard to expose.
The next step is to improve candidate exposure
inside forests while
retaining the nodewise feature-selection rule that makes the method distinctive.
