\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper evaluates conditional-inference tree and forest rankings for top-$k$
downstream prediction and proves a fixed-node guarantee for Stage~A permutation
testing. With the selected configurations, CIF ranks 4th of 17 methods in
classification and 3rd of 18 in regression. CIF also scores higher on average
than ctree, cforest, and CIT in the direct comparisons, with the clearest
evidence in classification.

The ablations show which measured CIF changes had the largest effects in this
setting. In the CIF runtime ablations, where each run changes one setting at a
time, disabling adaptive stopping and replacing histogram-256 thresholding with
exact threshold search produce the largest slowdowns. For ranking quality,
reducing CIF to one tree has the largest downstream score loss, while
split-count ranking and feature muting have smaller effects in these runs. The
high-$p$ and synthetic analyses add a practical diagnostic: CIF recovers
informative features more reliably within the top-$k$ list than at rank 1, and
candidate sampling can leave informative features unavailable at many
sparse-forest split decisions.

Taken together, the results support CIF as a practical top-$k$ feature-ranking
method for the evaluated downstream prediction tasks, especially in
classification. The practical guidance is to evaluate CIF rankings through the
downstream learner, choose $k$ by held-out performance, keep adaptive stopping
with bounded threshold search for similar ranking workloads, and monitor
candidate feature availability in sparse high-$p$ settings.
