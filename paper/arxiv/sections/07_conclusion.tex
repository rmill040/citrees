\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper isolates a principled core of conditional inference and shows how
to make it computationally practical. The theory gives a nodewise Stage~A
guarantee for fixed-$B$ feature selection rather than end-to-end calibration
for the adaptive learner. Across the within-CIF ablations, most runtime savings come
from adaptive stopping, with histogram thresholding as the main secondary
lever; feature scanning matters less once early stopping is active, and
threshold scanning, muting, and bootstrap have smaller or less consistent
effects on speed.

Empirically, the classification benchmark shows that CIF remains a useful
screening-and-ranking method after approximation and clearly improves on the historical
conditional-inference baselines. The boundary diagnostics clarify where that
pattern weakens: selected subsets are stronger than the exact head of the
ranking, and the
main sparse high-$p$ limit is forest-level exposure. Further progress likely
depends on improving that exposure
while preserving the nodewise feature-selection rule that makes the method
distinctive.
