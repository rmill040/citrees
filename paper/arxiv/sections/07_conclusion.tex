\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper studies how runtime hyperparameters in CIT and CIF affect
feature rankings, downstream prediction, and synthetic feature recovery. For
exhaustive fixed-$B$ Stage~A testing at a fixed node, with
a label-independent tested feature family, the Stage~A result gives Bonferroni
control of nodewise rejection under the complete nodewise permutation null.
That p-value interpretation applies to the local test; the full adaptive tree
and forest are evaluated empirically. In the CIF ablations, adaptive stopping
and histogram thresholding produce the largest timing changes. The CIT timing
results are mixed and are reported separately from the CIF runtime and
downstream-metric results.

On the classification benchmark, CIF ranks fourth of 17 methods with the
runtime hyperparameters studied here. In direct comparisons, its
average downstream scores are higher than ctree and cforest on the 22
classification datasets where those references were run, and higher than CIT
across 23 datasets.
The high-$p$ and synthetic results show two patterns: the synthetic results are
stronger for selected feature sets than for the first ranked feature, and in
sparse high-$p$ classification simulations, candidate sampling inside forests
can leave informative features unavailable at many split decisions. The
regression evidence is based on
fewer datasets and shows less consistent ranking gains. A natural next step is
to test variants that increase candidate feature coverage inside forests while
preserving the Stage~A/Stage~B separation that makes the method
distinctive.
